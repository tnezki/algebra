Unit 3 Practice by Depth of Knowledge

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2Students can recognize systems, solution types, ordered-pair solutions, boundary lines, shaded regions, and constraint vocabulary.

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, graph identification, ordered-pair check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize systems, identify solution behavior from graphs, verify ordered pairs, use system vocabulary, and identify boundary-line or shading features.

Assessment Ideas

  1. System Solution Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students identify system vocabulary, ordered-pair solutions, and solution types.
  2. Substitution and Elimination Readiness Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students identify equations prepared for substitution or elimination and verify simple ordered pairs.
  3. Linear Inequality Graph Features Check Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students identify boundary lines, shading, and test-point meaning.
  4. Constraint Vocabulary and Feasible Point Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students identify feasible regions, candidate points, and constraints.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need support with the language and visual meaning of systems, constraints, solution sets, and feasibility before moving into solving and modeling.

DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4Students can solve routine systems, graph linear inequalities, use test points, and connect algebraic and graphical solutions.

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, mini-quiz, graphing task, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can solve routine systems, graph inequalities, write systems from straightforward situations, and connect graphical and algebraic solution methods.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Graphing and Elimination Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students solve a system by graphing or elimination and verify the solution.
  2. Substitution Solving Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students solve systems using substitution and check ordered pairs.
  3. Writing Systems from Situations Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students define variables, write a system, solve, and interpret the solution.
  4. Graphing Inequalities and Feasible Region Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students graph inequalities, test points, and identify feasible solutions.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally solve routine systems and inequalities. These tasks reveal whether they can connect the solving process to graphs, contexts, and constraints.

DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6Students can justify solution methods, analyze solution behavior, critique reasoning, and explain constraints or feasible regions.

Format: Constructed response, strategy comparison, error analysis, graph interpretation, constraint analysis, or written explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can justify methods, analyze solution behavior, verify solutions, critique misconceptions, and connect systems or inequalities to context.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Solution Behavior Analysis Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students explain one solution, no solution, and infinitely many solutions using graphs and equations.
  2. Strategy Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students compare substitution, elimination, and graphing for efficiency and clarity.
  3. Context Interpretation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students interpret a system solution and evaluate whether it is reasonable.
  4. Feasible Region Justification Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students justify whether points satisfy all constraints and explain what the feasible region means.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand systems and constraints as connected representations rather than isolated procedures.

DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8Students can create, compare, critique, and revise systems or constraints to model decisions and justify conclusions.

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or decision-making task

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer systems and constraints to unfamiliar situations, design models, compare options, critique assumptions, and justify decisions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a System Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students create a context, write a system, solve it, and interpret the solution.
  2. Constraint Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students write inequalities, identify a feasible region, and justify possible solutions.
  3. Compare and Recommend Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students compare options using systems or inequalities and defend a recommendation.
  4. Critique and Revise Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students evaluate a flawed system or set of constraints and revise it so the model matches the situation.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can model, critique, revise, and justify systems and constraints. These are best as short performance checks rather than large projects.
