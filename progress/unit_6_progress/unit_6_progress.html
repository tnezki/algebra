Unit 6 Practice by Depth of Knowledge

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2Students can identify quadratic vocabulary, solution types, equation features, graph features, and formula components.

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, graph-feature check, formula identification, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, identify equation and graph features, recognize solution types, and set up solving methods before completing full procedures.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Zero Product Property Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students identify equations ready for the Zero Product Property and connect zeros to intercepts.
  2. Completing the Square Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students identify square structure, vertex form, and square-completion components.
  3. Quadratic Formula Setup Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students identify $a$, $b$, $c$, and the discriminant expression.
  4. Quadratic Model Feature Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students identify roots, intercepts, vertices, maximums, minimums, and context features.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need support with the language and visual meaning of quadratic solutions before deeper solving and modeling can occur.

  3. $x^2+3x=10$
  4. $x^2=16$

DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4Students can solve routine quadratic equations using factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula, then connect solutions to graphs or contexts.

Format: Exit ticket, mini-quiz, graph/equation task, method-practice task, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can execute quadratic solution methods and interpret routine graph or model information.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Solving by Factoring Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students solve by factoring and verify using substitution or graph features.
  2. Completing the Square Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students complete the square, solve, and connect the result to vertex form.
  3. Quadratic Formula Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students use the quadratic formula and interpret the number of real solutions.
  4. Quadratic Model Interpretation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students use a graph or equation to answer routine modeling questions.

Students at this level can generally execute solution methods and read quadratic models, but the assessment reveals whether they connect procedures to roots, vertices, and contexts.

DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6Students can justify solution choices, critique errors, compare methods, and explain how algebraic solutions connect to graph features.

Format: Constructed response, strategy comparison, error analysis, model interpretation, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can explain why methods work, critique mistakes, compare strategies, and connect solutions, vertices, and intercepts across representations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Zero Product Reasoning Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students explain why the equation must equal zero before using the Zero Product Property.
  2. Completing the Square Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students justify the square-completion step and connect it to graph features.
  3. Method Choice Defense Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students compare factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula for efficiency and reliability.
  4. Quadratic Model Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students critique an interpretation of roots, vertices, or predictions in context.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand quadratic methods as connected reasoning tools rather than isolated procedures.

DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8Students can design, critique, revise, and apply quadratic models to make and justify decisions in unfamiliar situations.

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or decision-making task

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer quadratic-solving tools to unfamiliar situations, select appropriate methods, build models, and justify decisions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Quadratic Equation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students design equations requiring different solution methods and justify why each method fits.
  2. Critique and Revise a Solution Method Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students critique a flawed method choice or algebraic solution and revise the reasoning.
  3. Projectile or Area Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students interpret roots, vertex, and constraints in a short contextual model.
  4. Quadratic Decision-Making Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students use a quadratic model to make and justify a recommendation.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, critique, model, and make decisions with quadratic representations. These are best as short performance checks rather than large projects.
1.
