Unit 5 Practice by Depth of Knowledge

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2Students can recognize factoring structures, vocabulary, quadratic forms, zeros, roots, intercepts, and graph features.

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, factor recognition, graph-feature check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, recognize factoring structures, identify quadratic graph features, and distinguish forms of quadratic expressions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. GCF and Factor Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students identify common factors, products, sums, terms, and factored forms.
  2. Trinomial Pattern Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students recognize factor pairs, trinomial structure, and area-model parts.
  3. Special Factoring Pattern Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students identify perfect square trinomials and differences of squares.
  4. Zeros and Quadratic Feature Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students identify zeros, intercepts, vertices, and useful forms from equations and graphs.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need support with the language of factors, zeros, and quadratic features before deeper reasoning about forms and graph behavior can occur.

DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4Students can factor expressions, verify factors, use area models, identify zeros from factored form, and connect equations to graphs.

Format: Exit ticket, mini-quiz, area-model task, graph/equation match, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can factor expressions, use area models, verify factors, identify zeros from factored form, and select useful forms for routine questions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. GCF and Structure Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students factor common factors and connect expanded and factored forms.
  2. Trinomial Factoring Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students factor trinomials using product-sum reasoning or area models.
  3. Special Pattern Factoring Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students factor special patterns and verify by multiplication.
  4. Factored Form and Zeros Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students identify zeros and connect them to x-intercepts and graphs.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally carry out factoring procedures and connect them to representations, but the assessment reveals whether they understand the structure behind the procedure.

DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6Students can justify factoring methods, analyze errors, connect algebraic forms to graph features, and defend useful forms.

Format: Constructed response, representation analysis, strategy comparison, error analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify factoring methods, analyze errors, connect algebraic forms to graph features, and defend which form is most useful.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Factoring Strategy Defense Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students compare factoring methods and justify an efficient strategy.
  2. Special Pattern Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students explain why a special pattern works or why it does not apply.
  3. Zeros and Graph Connection Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students justify how factored form, zeros, and x-intercepts represent the same information.
  4. Quadratic Form Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students compare standard, vertex, and factored form and defend which is most useful for a given question.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand factoring and quadratic forms as connected representations rather than isolated symbolic moves.

DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8Students can create, connect, compare, critique, and revise quadratic models using algebraic structure and graph features.

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer factoring and quadratic-form reasoning to unfamiliar situations, create models, and justify conclusions using multiple representations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Factoring Challenge Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students create a trinomial with intended factors and justify the construction.
  2. Special Pattern Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students critique and revise a flawed special-pattern argument.
  3. Zeros and Context Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students create a context where zeros or intercepts have meaning and explain limitations.
  4. Choose the Best Quadratic Form Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students select and defend the most useful form for answering different modeling questions.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, connect, critique, and revise quadratic models using algebraic structure and graph features. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.
1.

Design a polynomial expression with at least three terms that has a greatest common factor. Factor it completely and explain how you designed it.
