Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

A quadratic equation can sometimes be solved by factoring it into two linear factors. The zero product property says that if a product equals zero, then at least one factor must equal zero. This lets us split one quadratic equation into two simpler linear equations.

The solutions of a quadratic equation are also called zeros when the equation is written as a function equal to zero. On a graph, these same values appear as $x$-intercepts. Algebra, graphs, and tables are different ways to describe where the output is zero.

Factored form is useful because the zeros are visible from the factors. For example, $(x-2)(x+5)=0$ leads to $x=2$ or $x=-5$. Each factor gives one possible input value that makes the product zero.

In a real situation, not every algebraic solution is automatically meaningful. A negative time, length, or price may not fit the context even if it solves the equation. Good mathematical work explains both the number and its meaning.

This lesson connects factored equations, zeros, graphs, tables, and contexts. Strong solutions show the factoring, use the zero product property, and check whether the answers make sense for the situation.

Math Notes Zero Product Property

When a product equals zero, at least one factor must be zero.

$$(x-4)(x+3)=0$$ $$x-4=0\quad \text{or}\quad x+3=0$$
FactorSet equal to zeroSolution
$x-4$$x-4=0$$x=4$
$x+3$$x+3=0$$x=-3$

Context: If the factors describe break-even inputs, each zero is a possible break-even value that still needs to be interpreted.

Example 1: Use the Zero Product Property

Solve each equation by setting each factor equal to zero.

  1. $(x-4)(x+2)=0$
  2. $(3x-5)(x-6)=0$

Teacher answer: a) x=4 or x=-2. b) x=\frac{5}{3} or x=6. Reminder: ask students to justify each algebra move, not just state the answer.

You Try It 1: Use the Zero Product Property

Solve each equation by setting each factor equal to zero.

  1. $(x-8)(x+1)=0$
  2. $(2x+3)(x-5)=0$

Teacher answer: a) x=8 or x=-1. b) x=-\frac{3}{2} or x=5. Follow-up: have students compare the structure to the example before checking work.

Stop and Discuss

  1. How did the Example and You Try It both use the zero product property?
  2. Why must each factor be set equal to zero separately?

Teacher discussion: Both set each factor equal to zero; each factor gives a separate way for the product to be zero. Misconception watch: students may get a correct value without explaining what it means.

Example 2: Factor Standard Form

Solve by factoring.

$$x^2-9x+20=0$$
  1. Write the equation in factored form.
  2. Use the zero product property.
  3. State both solutions.

Teacher answer: x^2-9x+20=(x-5)(x-4), so x=5 or x=4. Reminder: ask students to justify each algebra move, not just state the answer.

You Try It 2: Factor Standard Form

Solve by factoring.

$$x^2-11x+24=0$$
  1. Write the equation in factored form.
  2. Use the zero product property.
  3. State both solutions.

Teacher answer: (x-3)(x-8)=0, so x=3 or x=8. Follow-up: have students compare the structure to the example before checking work.

Stop and Discuss

  1. What did factoring reveal that standard form did not show immediately?

Teacher discussion: Factoring revealed the zeros directly. Misconception watch: students may get a correct value without explaining what it means.

$$x(x+3)=70$$ $$x^2+3x-70=0$$

Context: After solving, only positive lengths make sense for the rectangle.

Example 3: Rectangle Area

A rectangle has area 70 square units. Its side lengths are $x$ and $x+3$.

  1. Write a quadratic equation for the area.
  2. Solve for the positive value of $x$.
  3. Explain why the other solution is not a possible side length.

Teacher answer: x(x+3)=70, so x^2+3x-70=0=(x-7)(x+10). The positive value is x=7; x=-10 is not a length. Reminder: ask students to justify each algebra move, not just state the answer.

You Try It 3: Rectangle Area

A rectangle has area 88 square units. Its side lengths are $x$ and $x+3$.

  1. Write a quadratic equation for the area.
  2. Solve for the positive value of $x$.
  3. Explain why the other solution is not possible.

Teacher answer: x(x+3)=88, so x^2+3x-88=0=(x-8)(x+11). Positive value: x=8. Follow-up: have students compare the structure to the example before checking work.

Stop and Discuss

  1. Why was only the positive value reasonable in the rectangle problems?

Teacher discussion: Lengths cannot be negative in the rectangle context. Misconception watch: students may get a correct value without explaining what it means.

Math Notes Checking Factor Signs

A sign error in factoring changes both the middle term and the solutions.

$$x^2-4x-21=(x-7)(x+3)$$
Proposed factorsProduct termMiddle term
$(x-3)(x-7)$$21$$-10x$
$(x-7)(x+3)$$-21$$-4x$

A correct critique checks that the factors reproduce both the constant term and the middle term.

Example 4: Critique a Factoring Error

A student says the solutions of $x^2-4x-21=0$ are $x=3$ and $x=7$ because the factors are $(x-3)(x-7)$.

  1. Identify the factoring error.
  2. Correct the factorization and solutions.
  3. Explain how the middle term reveals the mistake.

Teacher answer: (x-3)(x-7)=x^2-10x+21, not x^2-4x-21. Correct: (x-7)(x+3)=0, so x=7 or x=-3. Reminder: ask students to justify each algebra move, not just state the answer.

You Try It 4: Critique a Factoring Error

A student says the solutions of $x^2+2x-24=0$ are $x=-4$ and $x=-6$ because the factors are $(x+4)(x+6)$.

  1. Identify the factoring error.
  2. Correct the factorization and solutions.
  3. Explain how the constant term reveals the mistake.

Teacher answer: (x+4)(x+6)=x^2+10x+24. Correct: (x+6)(x-4)=0, so x=-6 or x=4. Follow-up: have students compare the structure to the example before checking work.

Stop and Discuss

  1. How did checking the middle term help find the factoring error?

Teacher discussion: Expanding the factors checks whether the product and middle term match the original quadratic. Misconception watch: students may get a correct value without explaining what it means.

Summary

Today you solved quadratic equations by factoring and using the zero product property.

Zeros, solutions, and $x$-intercepts all point to input values where the quadratic output is zero.

A common error is factoring with the wrong signs and then trusting the incorrect zeros.

Strong understanding means you can solve, check the factors, and explain whether each solution makes sense in context.

Common Mistakes

  • Setting the whole product equal to one factor. Why it is tempting: the factors look like separate equations already. What to check instead: set each factor equal to zero.
  • Wrong factor signs. Why it is tempting: numbers may multiply correctly but add incorrectly. What to check instead: expand or check product and sum.
  • Keeping a negative length. Why it is tempting: algebra can produce two numbers. What to check instead: decide whether the context allows negative values.
  • Calling zeros and intercepts different answers. Why it is tempting: the words sound unrelated. What to check instead: connect zeros to $x$-intercepts where $y=0$.
  • Stopping before interpretation. Why it is tempting: solving feels complete. What to check instead: write what each solution means.
