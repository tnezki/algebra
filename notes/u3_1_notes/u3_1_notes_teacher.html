Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

Many real situations have two rules that must be true at the same time. In a graph of a system, the intersection point is the ordered pair that satisfies both equations. That point can represent a break-even amount, a shared cost, or another result where two plans match.

Sometimes a graph gives a helpful picture, but algebra can give an exact answer. Elimination works by adding or subtracting equations so one variable disappears. Then you solve for the remaining variable and use it to find the complete ordered pair.

Two lines can meet once, never meet, or lie exactly on top of each other. When you compare slopes and intercepts, you can often predict the solution type before doing all the solving. The graph helps you check whether the algebraic result makes sense.

Useful representations include two-line graphs, tables of paired values, standard form equations, and written solution-type statements. Strong work connects the intersection, the algebra, and the meaning of the ordered pair.

In a context, a system often compares two plans or two conditions. The solution is not just a point; it tells what input makes both rules true and what output happens at that input.

Example 1: Interpret an Intersection

Two school clubs compare their banner costs with $y=x+2$ and $y=-x+8$, where $x$ is the number of banners and $y$ is total cost in tens of dollars.

  1. Find the ordered pair where the two rules have the same output.
  2. Explain what that ordered pair means for the two clubs.
  3. Would $(4,6)$ be a solution to both equations? Explain how you know.

Example 1 answer: Intersection: $(3,5)$. It means both costs are 50 dollars for 3 banners. $(4,6)$ only satisfies $y=x+2$, not $y=-x+8$.
Teacher reminder: Ask students to name the representation they used before they describe the procedure.

You Try It 1: Interpret an Intersection

Two fundraising options are modeled by $y=4x+1$ and $y=2x+7$.

  1. Find the ordered pair where the two rules have the same output.
  2. Explain what the ordered pair means.
  3. Decide whether $(2,9)$ satisfies both rules.

You Try It 1 answer: $4x+1=2x+7$, so $x=3$ and $y=13$. It means both options match at that input and output. $(2,9)$ satisfies the first but not the second.

Example 3: Set Equal Before Graphing

Two expressions give the same output at the intersection.

$$\begin{aligned}y&=5x-1\\y&=-x+11\end{aligned}$$
  1. Set the expressions equal and solve for $x$.
  2. Find the matching $y$-value.
  3. Describe how a graph could check your ordered pair.

Example 3 answer: $5x-1=-x+11$, so $6x=12$ and $x=2$. Then $y=9$. A graph should show the lines crossing at $(2,9)$.
Teacher reminder: Ask students to name the representation they used before they describe the procedure.

Math Notes Elimination Produces One Variable First

Elimination combines equations so one variable cancels, but the first value you find is only part of the ordered pair.

$$\begin{aligned}x+2y&=13\\-x+y&=2\\3y&=15\end{aligned}$$

After $y=5$, substitute into either original equation: $x+2(5)=13$, so $x=3$.

Context: A complete system solution needs both coordinates, written as $(3,5)$.

Example 4: Critique an Elimination Step

A student adds the equations and says the solution is $y=5$.

$$\begin{aligned}x+2y&=13\\-x+y&=2\end{aligned}$$
  1. Add the equations and identify what value is found first.
  2. Find the missing coordinate.
  3. Critique the student claim in one complete sentence.

Example 4 answer: Adding gives $3y=15$, so $y=5$. Substitute: $x+10=13$, so $x=3$. The complete solution is $(3,5)$; $y=5$ alone is incomplete.
Teacher reminder: Ask students to name the representation they used before they describe the procedure.

You Try It 4: Critique an Elimination Step

Summary

Today you connected graphical and algebraic ways to solve systems. A system solution must satisfy both equations at the same time.

On a graph, one solution appears as one intersection point; no solution appears as parallel lines; infinitely many solutions appear as the same line.

A common error is stopping after finding only one coordinate during elimination.

Strong understanding means you can solve, classify the solution type, and explain what the solution means in a situation.

Common Mistakes

  • Stopping after one coordinate. Why it is tempting: elimination often gives $x$ or $y$ first. What to check instead: substitute back to write a complete ordered pair.
  • Reading the wrong crossing point. Why it is tempting: nearby gridlines can look close. What to check instead: verify the point in both equations.
  • Mixing solution types. Why it is tempting: parallel and identical lines both do not have one crossing point. What to check instead: compare slopes and intercepts carefully.
  • Changing equations during elimination. Why it is tempting: multiplying or adding can feel like changing the problem. What to check instead: only use equivalent equations and keep both constraints.
  • Forgetting context. Why it is tempting: the ordered pair can look like just numbers. What to check instead: name what each coordinate means.
