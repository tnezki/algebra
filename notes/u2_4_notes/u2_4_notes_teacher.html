Comparing Linear Representations
Compare linear relationships by translating different forms into slope and starting value.

Mathematical idea: Two linear relationships can be compared fairly when their rates and starting values are made visible.

Today you will compare equations, tables, contexts, and graph descriptions. You will use slope and intercept evidence to support conclusions about which relationship changes faster or starts higher.

Learning Target: Students will analyze and compare linear relationships using multiple representations.

Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

Comparing linear relationships is easier when you focus on features. Slope and starting value are the two most useful features because they describe repeated change and the amount present at the beginning. Those features can be found in equations, tables, graphs, and contexts.

Different representations can still describe the same kind of information. A table can show slope through repeated output changes, while an equation shows slope as the coefficient of $x$. Once both forms reveal slope and starting value, they can be compared fairly.

Some choices depend on the input value. A plan with a low starting cost but a high rate may be cheaper at first and more expensive later. Students need to compare both the intercept and the slope instead of relying on only one feature.

Graphs help show when one relationship grows faster than another. A steeper positive line increases more quickly, while a steeper negative line decreases more quickly. The direction and magnitude both matter.

Strong comparisons include evidence. Instead of saying one line is better or faster, name the slope, starting value, and at least one tested input value. That makes the conclusion checkable.

Example 1: Compare Slope Magnitudes

Relationship A has slope $4$. Relationship B has slope $-6$.

  1. Which relationship changes faster in magnitude?
  2. Which relationship is increasing?
  3. Explain why sign and magnitude are different ideas.

Teacher answer key: B changes faster in magnitude; A increases; sign gives direction and magnitude gives size. Misconception watch: require students to name the representation evidence they used.

  3. Explain why sign and magnitude are different ideas.

Teacher answer key: B changes faster in magnitude; A decreases; sign is direction and magnitude is size. Follow-up: ask one student to explain how this parallels the example without copying steps blindly.

Summary

Comparing linear representations means translating each relationship into common features.

Slope tells repeated change and intercept tells starting value, even when the relationship is shown as a table, graph, equation, or context.

A common error is choosing the greater starting value without checking the slope.

Strong understanding means supporting comparison claims with numerical evidence from each representation.

Common Mistakes

  • Comparing only one feature. Why it is tempting: one number may stand out. What to check instead: check both slope and starting value.
  • Ignoring negative signs. Why it is tempting: larger absolute values look like larger outputs. What to check instead: separate direction from magnitude.
  • Assuming cheaper stays cheaper. Why it is tempting: one tested input may be persuasive. What to check instead: test the input asked about and consider slope.
  • Reading table slope from adjacent outputs only. Why it is tempting: input steps may not be 1. What to check instead: divide by the input change.
  • Using graph appearance alone. Why it is tempting: scale can change steepness visually. What to check instead: use coordinates or equations for evidence.
