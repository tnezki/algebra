Rate of Change and Slope
Use slope to describe how one quantity changes for each step in another quantity.

Mathematical idea: Slope compares vertical change to horizontal change, so it tells the constant rate in a graph, table, or situation.

Today you will calculate slope from points, tables, and contexts. You will also decide whether a slope statement makes sense for the situation it describes.

Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

Linear relationships show up whenever two quantities change together at a steady pace. You can see slope in a graph, a table, or a real situation such as speed, cost per item, or water level change. Each representation gives evidence about how much one quantity changes when the other quantity changes.

A slope calculation is a comparison, not just a number to memorize. The ratio compares vertical change to horizontal change, often written as $\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$. That means the units matter because the numerator and denominator usually describe different quantities.

Some rates increase and some rates decrease. A tank draining water can have a negative slope because the output amount goes down as time goes up. The sign of the slope should match the direction of change in the situation, not just the position of the points.

Tables are useful when input values change by equal steps. If the output changes by the same amount each time, the relationship has a constant rate of change. Coordinate graphs show the same idea through steepness and direction.

Strong slope reasoning connects numbers to meaning. A slope of $3$ might mean 3 dollars per ticket, 3 miles per hour, or 3 centimeters per week depending on the labels. Always name what the change is measuring.

Summary

Slope describes constant rate of change in student-friendly language: how much the output changes for each input step.

The main notation is $m=\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$, and the order of subtraction must match in numerator and denominator.

A common error is deciding slope sign from where a point sits instead of from how the output changes.

Strong understanding means naming the rate with units and using values, graphs, or context to justify whether the slope is reasonable.

Common Mistakes

  • Subtracting in different directions. Why it is tempting: students may compute quickly from two points. What to check instead: subtract the second coordinate minus the first coordinate in both numerator and denominator.
  • Ignoring units. Why it is tempting: the number alone feels complete. What to check instead: state output units per input unit.
  • Using only steepness. Why it is tempting: a line may look steep on a scaled graph. What to check instead: use exact changes from coordinates or table values.
  • Confusing negative slope with negative values. Why it is tempting: negative coordinates look connected to negative slope. What to check instead: check whether outputs decrease as inputs increase.
  • Assuming all points fit. Why it is tempting: three points may look close. What to check instead: compare slopes between pairs.
