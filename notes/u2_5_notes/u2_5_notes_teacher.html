Linear Modeling with Data
Use scatter plots and lines of fit to model real-world data.

Mathematical idea: A linear model can summarize a data trend, but predictions must be judged for reasonableness.

Today you will describe association, estimate with a line of fit, and build approximate models from data. You will also decide when a linear model is useful enough for the question being asked.

Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

Scatter plots show patterns in data pairs. A positive association appears when the points tend to rise from left to right. That means larger input values are usually connected with larger output values.

A line of fit is a simple model for the overall trend. It helps you estimate values that are not listed exactly and interpret an average rate of change. The line does not need to pass through every point to be useful.

Predictions depend on how close the new input is to the collected data. Estimating near the data range usually uses the observed pattern, while predicting far outside the range can be risky. The farther the input is from the data, the more carefully the model should be questioned.

Tables and scatter plots work together in modeling. Tables show exact data values, while scatter plots show the shape and strength of the trend. A reasonable model should respect both views.

Strong data modeling includes a reasonableness check. Look for outliers, curved patterns, or places where the trend changes. A line can be helpful even when it is not perfect, but the model should fit the question being asked.

Example 2: Predict with a Line of Fit

A line of fit for plant height is $h=3w+20$, where $w$ is weeks and $h$ is height in centimeters.

  1. Predict the height at week 9.
  2. Interpret the slope in context.
  3. Explain whether week 9 is a reasonable prediction if data were collected through week 8.

Teacher answer key: $h(9)=47$ cm; slope is 3 cm per week; week 9 is close to week 8, so the prediction is reasonable but still an estimate. Misconception watch: require students to name the representation evidence they used.

Summary

Linear modeling with data means using a line to summarize a trend in scattered real-world points.

Scatter plots show association, and a line of fit gives an approximate equation for prediction and interpretation.

A common error is treating a line of fit like it must pass through every data point.

Strong understanding means making predictions, interpreting slope in context, and explaining whether the model is reasonable for the question.

Common Mistakes

  • Calling every rising pattern linear. Why it is tempting: positive association can still curve. What to check instead: check whether a straight line fits well enough.
  • Forcing the line through every point. Why it is tempting: equations in earlier units often matched exactly. What to check instead: remember data models are approximate.
  • Using an outlier to set the line. Why it is tempting: an extreme point is easy to notice. What to check instead: choose points near the middle of the trend.
  • Extrapolating too far. Why it is tempting: the equation continues algebraically. What to check instead: ask whether the input is near the data range.
  • Forgetting context units. Why it is tempting: slope from data can feel abstract. What to check instead: name output units per input unit.
