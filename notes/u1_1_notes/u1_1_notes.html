Multiple Representations of Relationships
Represent the same relationship with tables, graphs, equations, and contexts, then use evidence to connect them.

Mathematical idea: A relationship can look different in each representation but still describe the same input-output pattern.

Students will connect the section target to examples, try-it problems, and a short exit ticket. By the end, they should be able to name the evidence that supports their representation or model.

Learning Target

Learning Target: Students will connect tables, graphs, equations, and contexts that represent the same relationship.

I can statements

  • I can construct a table, graph, equation, or context from another representation of the same relationship.
  • I can use features in one representation to work with a related table, graph, equation, or context.
  • I can determine whether two representations belong to the same relationship using evidence from values, rates, or patterns.

Summary

Today you practiced this target in student language: Students will connect tables, graphs, equations, and contexts that represent the same relationship.

The key habit is to name the quantities and use the representation that gives the clearest evidence about multiple representations of relationships.

A common error is to copy numbers or symbols without checking what they mean in the situation.

Strong understanding means you can explain your choice, connect it to evidence, and check whether the result makes sense.

Common Mistakes

  • Swapping input and output. Why it is tempting: the numbers may both appear in every representation. What to check instead: name what is chosen first and what depends on it.
  • Matching by appearance only. Why it is tempting: a graph or table can look familiar. What to check instead: check values, rates, and starting values.
  • Ignoring units. Why it is tempting: numbers may be copied without meaning. What to check instead: attach units to each quantity.
  • Using one point as full evidence. Why it is tempting: one matching point feels convincing. What to check instead: check at least two values or the rule.
  • Forgetting the starting value. Why it is tempting: the repeated change gets attention first. What to check instead: look for the value when the input is \$0$.
