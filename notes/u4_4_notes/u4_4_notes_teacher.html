Polynomial Operations
Use structure, like terms, and models to add, subtract, and multiply polynomials.

Mathematical idea: Polynomial operations create equivalent expressions when each term is handled by its structure.

Students will identify terms, combine like terms, and multiply binomials with area models. They will connect symbolic operations to area and expression contexts.

Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

Polynomial expressions are built from terms. Like terms have the same variable part and exponent, so their coefficients can be combined. For example, $3x$ and $-5x$ are like terms, but $3x^2$ and $3x$ are not.

An area model organizes multiplication by showing every product in a rectangle. Each row label multiplies each column label, so no product is skipped. After the products are listed, like terms can be combined to write an equivalent expression.

Sometimes structure tells useful information before any full expansion. In $(x+4)(x-6)$, the constant term comes from $4\cdot(-6)$, and the middle term comes from the two $x$ products. This can help predict signs and catch mistakes.

Polynomial operations can be represented with standard form, factored form, tables, area models, and algebra tiles. Each representation shows the same expression in a different way.

In contexts, polynomial expressions may describe area, perimeter, or total change. The expression must make sense with the story, especially when side lengths or quantities need to be positive.

Math Notes Terms, Coefficients, and Like Terms

Before operating with polynomials, identify the parts of the expression.

Quadratic term

The term with $x^2$, such as $3x^2$.

Linear term

The term with $x$, such as $-5x$.

Constant term

The number term, such as $7$.

Like terms

Terms with the same variable part and exponent.

Example: In $3x^2-5x+7$, the coefficient of the linear term is $-5$.

Example 1: Identify Polynomial Terms

In $-4x^2+6x-8$, identify the quadratic term, linear term, and constant term.

Answer: Quadratic term $-4x^2$, linear term $6x$, constant term $-8$.

You Try It 1: Name the Parts

In $5x^2-2x+9$, identify the quadratic term, linear term, and constant term.

Answer: Quadratic term $5x^2$, linear term $-2x$, constant term $9$.

Stop and Discuss

  1. Why is the sign part of a term?

Discussion guide: The sign tells whether the term is added or subtracted; changing it changes the expression.

Math Notes Area Models for Binomial Products

An area model shows every partial product in a binomial multiplication.

The model represents $(x+3)(x+5)$, so the four products are $x^2$, $5x$, $3x$, and $15$.

Combining like terms gives $$x^2+5x+3x+15=x^2+8x+15.$$

In an area context, each cell is part of the total area.

Example 2: Multiply with an Area Model

Use the area model to write the expanded form of $(x+2)(x+6)$. Then combine like terms.

$x$$6$
$x$$x^2$$6x$
$2$$2x$$12$

Answer: $x^2+6x+2x+12=x^2+8x+12$.

You Try It 2: Area Model Practice

Use the area model to expand $(x+4)(x+1)$ and combine like terms.

$x$$1$
$x$$x^2$$x$
$4$$4x$$4$

Answer: $x^2+x+4x+4=x^2+5x+4$.

Stop and Discuss

  1. Which cell products combine as like terms?

Discussion guide: The $x$-terms combine; $x^2$ and constants do not combine with them.

Example 3: List Products and Combine

Use the row and column labels to list the four products for $(x-3)(x+5)$, then combine like terms.

$x$$5$
$x$
$-3$

Answer: Products are $x^2$, $5x$, $-3x$, and $-15$; combined form is $x^2+2x-15$.

You Try It 3: Complete the Products

Use the row and column labels to list the four products for $(x+6)(x-2)$, then combine like terms.

$x$$-2$
$x$
$6$

Answer: Products are $x^2$, $-2x$, $6x$, and $-12$; combined form is $x^2+4x-12$.

Stop and Discuss

  1. How does an empty area model help you organize the four products?

Discussion guide: Each row-column intersection is one product, so the model prevents missing a term.

Example 4: Predict Structure Before Expanding

Analyze the structure of $(x+7)(x-2)$ before multiplying. Predict the sign of the constant term and explain whether the middle term will be positive or negative.

Answer: Constant term is negative because $7\cdot(-2)=-14$. Middle term is positive because $7x-2x=5x$.

You Try It 4: Use Structure to Predict

Analyze $(x-5)(x-3)$ before multiplying. Predict the sign of the constant term and the sign of the middle term.

Answer: Constant term is positive because $(-5)(-3)=15$. Middle term is negative because $-5x-3x=-8x$.

Stop and Discuss

  1. How can predicting signs help you catch an expansion error?

Discussion guide: Compare your predicted constant and middle-term signs with the final expression.

Summary

Today you identified polynomial terms and used structure to operate with polynomial expressions. You connected symbolic products to area models.

Like terms have the same variable part and exponent, and area models show all partial products before like terms are combined.

A common error is dropping a negative sign or combining terms that are not like terms.

Strong understanding means you can move among factored form, expanded form, and a visual model while keeping the expression equivalent.

Common Mistakes

  • Dropping signs. Why it is tempting: students focus on numbers and variables. What to check instead: treat the sign as part of the term.
  • Combining unlike terms. Why it is tempting: simplifying feels like everything should combine. What to check instead: only combine terms with the same variable part and exponent.
  • Skipping a partial product. Why it is tempting: mental multiplication is fast. What to check instead: use four products or an area model.
  • Misreading area labels. Why it is tempting: row and column labels can look decorative. What to check instead: multiply every row label by every column label.
  • Ignoring context restrictions. Why it is tempting: algebraic expressions can be manipulated alone. What to check instead: check whether lengths or quantities make sense in the situation.
