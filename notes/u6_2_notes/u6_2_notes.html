Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

Completing the square rewrites part of a quadratic as a perfect-square trinomial. For $x^2+bx$, the needed constant is $(b/2)^2$. This creates a square like $(x+4)^2$ or $(x-5)^2$.

When an equation is solved by completing the square, both sides must stay balanced. If a constant is added to one side, it must also be added to the other side. The goal is to make one side a perfect square that can be solved with square roots.

Completing the square also connects standard form to vertex form. Vertex form shows the vertex and axis of symmetry directly. This makes it useful when the maximum or minimum is more important than the zeros.

Area models can help explain why the extra constant is needed. The middle term is split into two equal parts, and the missing corner completes the square. The algebraic constant matches the area of that missing corner.

This lesson uses equations, vertex form, tables, graphs, and area models. Strong work keeps the equation balanced and explains what the new form reveals.

Math Notes Solving by Completing the Square

To solve, move the constant, add the completing-square constant to both sides, then take square roots.

$$x^2+6x-7=0$$ $$x^2+6x=7$$ $$x^2+6x+9=16$$ $$(x+3)^2=16$$

Now square roots give $x+3=\pm4$, so $x=1$ or $x=-7$.

Summary

Today you completed the square to solve equations and rewrite quadratics in vertex form.

The key constant is $(b/2)^2$, and it must be used in a way that keeps expressions or equations equivalent.

A common error is adding the completing-square constant without balancing the equation.

Strong understanding means you can connect the algebra to perfect squares, vertex form, and graph features.

Common Mistakes

  • Using $b^2$ instead of $(b/2)^2$. Why it is tempting: the middle coefficient is visible. What to check instead: take half first, then square.
  • Adding to only one side. Why it is tempting: the added constant seems like part of a pattern. What to check instead: keep the equation balanced.
  • Factoring the perfect square incorrectly. Why it is tempting: signs can disappear inside parentheses. What to check instead: match the sign of half of $b$.
  • Forgetting the outside adjustment in vertex form. Why it is tempting: adding inside changes the expression. What to check instead: subtract the same amount when rewriting an expression.
  • Misreading the vertex. Why it is tempting: the form has subtraction signs. What to check instead: use $(x-h)^2+k$ to name $(h,k)$.
