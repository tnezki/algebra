Expressions and Structure
Teacher Notes
Looking inside expressions to see how they are built.

Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by highlighting important ideas, circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

An algebraic expression is a math phrase. It can have numbers, variables, operation symbols, and grouping symbols. Expressions do not have an equals sign because they do not make a complete statement yet.

Structure means how something is built. In an expression, structure helps us see the parts. For example, in $4x+7$, the term $4x$ changes when $x$ changes, but the $7$ stays the same. In $3(x+2)$, the parentheses show a group being repeated three times.

Sometimes two expressions look different but have the same value for every input. These are called equivalent expressions. We can rewrite expressions by combining like terms or using the distributive property.

The goal is not just to “get an answer.” The goal is to understand what the expression is showing so we can choose a helpful form and explain why it works.

Stop and Discuss

  1. What does it mean to look at the structure of an expression?
  2. Why might two expressions look different but still be equivalent?
  3. Why is rewriting an expression sometimes more helpful than evaluating it right away?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • Terms are pieces separated by addition/subtraction.
  • Coefficients multiply variables.
  • Constants are numbers without variables.
  • Like terms can be combined because they have the same variable part.
🔴 Teacher Note: Redirect if students call every number a coefficient.

Expressions Have Parts That Do Different Jobs

Before rewriting an expression, we need to notice its parts. Terms are separated by addition or subtraction. A coefficient is the number multiplying a variable. A constant is a number without a variable.

Term: a part of an expression separated by addition or subtraction.

Coefficient: the number multiplying a variable.

Constant: a number that does not have a variable attached.

Example 1

Identify the terms, coefficients, and constant in the expression.

$$6x+9-2x+5$$

terms coefficients constant terms like terms

Then explain which parts could be combined and why.

🔴 Complete worked solution:
  • Terms: $6x$, $9$, $-2x$, $5$.
  • Coefficients: $6$ and $-2$.
  • Constant terms: $9$ and $5$.
  • Like terms: $6x$ and $-2x$; $9$ and $5$.
  • Combine: $6x-2x=4x$ and $9+5=14$, so the expression simplifies to $4x+14$.
🔴 Teacher Note: Emphasize keeping subtraction signs attached to the term.

You Try It 1

Identify the terms, coefficients, and constant terms in $8n-3+4n+10$. Then circle the like terms in your work.

🔴 Answer:
  • Terms: $8n$, $-3$, $4n$, $10$.
  • Coefficients: $8$ and $4$.
  • Constant terms: $-3$ and $10$.
  • Like terms: $8n$ and $4n$; $-3$ and $10$.
  • Simplified expression: $12n+7$.
🔴 Teacher Note: Common misconception: dropping the negative sign on $-3$.

Stop and Discuss

  1. Why is $6x$ a term but $6$ is the coefficient?
  2. How can recognizing like terms help before you simplify?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • $6x$ is a term because it is a full piece of the expression; $6$ is the coefficient because it multiplies $x$.
  • Recognizing like terms helps decide what can be combined.
🔴 Teacher Note: Connect vocabulary to simplifying, not memorization.

Like Terms Can Be Combined

Like terms have the same variable part. Combining like terms means adding or subtracting the coefficients while keeping the variable part the same.

Like terms: terms with matching variable parts, such as $5x$ and $2x$, or $9$ and $-4$.

Example 2

Simplify the expression and explain which terms were combined.

$$5x+3+2x-1$$

Use the structure to group variable terms with variable terms and constants with constants.

🔴 Complete worked solution:

$5x+3+2x-1=(5x+2x)+(3-1)=7x+2$.

Combined $5x$ and $2x$ because they are like variable terms; combined $3$ and $-1$ because they are constants.

🔴 Teacher Note: What students usually do wrong: combine $3$ with $2x$ or lose the negative on $-1$.

You Try It 2

Simplify $7a-4+3a+9$. In one sentence, explain why the variable terms can combine but the variable terms and constants should not combine together.

🔴 Answer:

$7a-4+3a+9=(7a+3a)+(-4+9)=10a+5$.

The variable terms can combine because they both have $a$; constants do not have the same variable part as the $a$-terms.

🔴 Teacher Note: Quick CFU: “What variable part does each term have?”

Parentheses Show a Group

The distributive property lets us rewrite a group being multiplied. It does not create a new relationship. It creates an equivalent expression that shows the same value in a different form.

Distributive property: $a(b+c)=ab+ac$.

Example 3

Use the area model to explain why $4(x+6)$ is equivalent to $4x+24$.

height $4$
$x$ by $4$ gives $4x$$6$ by $4$ gives $24$

Then write a sentence connecting the picture to the symbols.

🔴 Complete worked solution:

The area model shows two rectangles with the same height $4$. One has width $x$, giving area $4x$. The other has width $6$, giving area $24$. Together, the total area is $4x+24$, so $4(x+6)=4x+24$.

🔴 Teacher Note: Emphasize that distribution creates an equivalent expression, not a new value.

You Try It 3

Rewrite $3(y+5)$ using the distributive property. Then explain what each new term came from.

🔴 Answer:

$3(y+5)=3y+15$.

The $3y$ comes from $3\cdot y$ and the $15$ comes from $3\cdot5$.

🔴 Teacher Note: Common misconception: writing $3y+5$ by multiplying only the first term.

Stop and Discuss

  1. Why must the outside number multiply every term inside the parentheses?
  2. How does an area model help show equivalent expressions instead of just memorizing a rule?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • The outside number must multiply every term because the whole group is being multiplied.
  • An area model shows each part of the group getting multiplied, which explains why the expressions are equivalent.
🔴 Teacher Note: Redirect “just move the number in” to “multiply each term in the group.”

Different Forms Can Be Useful for Different Reasons

An expression can be written in more than one equivalent form. One form may show the story better. Another form may make substitution or mental math easier.

Example 4

A snack box has $2$ bags of pretzels and $2$ juice boxes. Each pretzel bag costs $p$ dollars and each juice box costs $3$ dollars.

Grouped form

$2(p+3)$

Shows two identical snack sets.

Expanded form

$2p+6$

Shows pretzel cost and juice cost separately.

  1. Explain why the expressions are equivalent.
  2. Which form best shows the story?
  3. Which form would be easier to evaluate if $p=4$?
🔴 Complete worked solution:
  • The expressions are equivalent because $2(p+3)=2p+6$.
  • Grouped form best shows the story: two identical snack sets.
  • If $p=4$, either works: $2(4+3)=14$ or $2(4)+6=14$. Expanded form may be easier for seeing separate costs.
🔴 Teacher Note: Key point: different equivalent forms reveal different information.

You Try It 4

A party favor bag has $5$ stickers and $5$ erasers. Each sticker costs $s$ cents and each eraser costs $8$ cents.

  1. Write a grouped expression.
  2. Write an expanded expression.
  3. Explain what each form helps you see.
🔴 Answer:
  • Grouped expression: $5(s+8)$.
  • Expanded expression: $5s+40$.
  • Grouped form shows $5$ identical bags; expanded form shows sticker cost and eraser cost separately.
🔴 Teacher Note: Extension: ask students to evaluate both forms when $s=6$ and compare.

Stop and Discuss

  1. Why might a teacher ask for an explanation instead of only a simplified answer?
  2. When might the unsimplified form be more useful than the simplified form?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • Explanations show whether the expression matches the story and whether the student understands structure.
  • The unsimplified form may be useful when it shows groups, repeated sets, or the original context more clearly.
🔴 Teacher Note: Connect to modeling: form should serve meaning, not only simplification.

Summary

  • An expression is a math phrase without an equals sign.
  • Structure means how an expression is built.
  • Terms are separated by addition or subtraction.
  • Like terms have matching variable parts and can be combined.
  • The distributive property rewrites a grouped expression into an equivalent expanded form.
  • Equivalent expressions may look different but have the same value for every input.
  • Different forms can reveal different information about the same situation.

Common Mistakes

  • Combining unlike terms: $3x+4$ cannot become $7x$ because $3x$ and $4$ are different kinds of parts.
  • Dropping a negative sign: In $6x-2+3x$, the $-2$ stays negative when terms are rearranged.
  • Distributing to only one term: $4(x+6)$ becomes $4x+24$, not $4x+6$.
  • Thinking equivalent means identical-looking: Equivalent expressions can look different and still always have the same value.
  • Simplifying without meaning: A simplified form is useful, but students should still know what the parts represent.
  • Choosing one form for every situation: Different forms can be helpful for different reasons.
