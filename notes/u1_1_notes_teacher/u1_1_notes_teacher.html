Section 1.1
Multiple Representations of Relationships
Teacher Notes
Seeing the same relationship in more than one way.

Mathematical idea: A relationship can look different in a table, graph, equation, or story, but the pattern underneath can still be the same.

Today we will practice connecting representations, explaining what changes, and using evidence to decide whether two representations match.

Learning Target

Students will connect tables, graphs, equations, and contexts that represent the same relationship.

I can statements

  • I can identify when different representations describe the same relationship.
  • I can match tables, graphs, equations, and situations.
  • I can describe how two quantities are related.
  • I can determine what quantities are changing.
  • I can justify how representations are connected.

Future Task

A school walkathon raises a starting donation plus the same amount for each mile walked. Three students claim they have representations for the same relationship.

Context

The team already has \$10 in donations. They earn \$5 more for each mile walked.

Equation

$d=5m+10$, where $m$ is miles and $d$ is dollars.

Table

Miles0123
Dollars10152025

Graph

  1. Use at least three pieces of evidence to justify whether the representations match.
  2. Explain what the starting donation means in each representation.
  3. Explain what the amount per mile means in each representation.
  4. A classmate says the table must be wrong because the miles do not start at $1$. Write a response that corrects the misunderstanding.
🔴 Sample student responses / Teacher Note:
  • All four representations match because they show the same start value, $10$, and the same increase, $5$ per mile.
  • The starting donation appears as $+10$ in the equation, $10$ dollars at $m=0$ in the table, and the graph's starting height.
  • The amount per mile appears as the coefficient $5$, the table increasing by $5$, and the graph rising at a steady rate.
  • Response to classmate: Starting at $0$ miles makes sense because the team can have donations before anyone walks.
🔴 Teacher Note: Key point: students should justify matches with evidence across representations, not just say “they look the same.”

Intro

Directions

Read the introduction aloud with a partner or in a small group. As you read, annotate the text by marking important ideas, highlighting or circling key vocabulary, and writing short notes about connections, questions, or predictions in the margins.

A relationship tells how two things are connected. In math, the two things are often quantities that can change. For example, the number of rides on a bus and the total cost can be connected.

We can show the same relationship in different ways. A table shows organized input and output values. A graph shows the pattern as points or a line. An equation uses symbols to describe the rule. A context tells the story in words.

The important question is not, “Do these look the same?” The important question is, “Do these show the same relationship?” To answer that, we look for evidence. We check what starts the pattern, what changes each time, and what the numbers mean.

When you connect representations, you are building a stronger picture of the relationship. One representation may be easier to read than another, but they should all agree about how the two quantities are related.

Stop and Discuss

  1. What is a relationship in math?
  2. How can a table, graph, equation, and context show the same idea?
  3. What evidence could help you decide whether two representations match?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • A relationship shows how two quantities are connected.
  • A table, graph, equation, and context can show the same inputs, outputs, start value, and change.
  • Evidence could include matching input-output pairs, the same starting value, and the same rate of change.
🔴 Teacher Note: Listen for students moving beyond surface features. Redirect if students say representations match only because “they both go up.”

One Relationship, Four Ways

Each representation can tell part of the story. When they match, the numbers and the meaning should agree.

Input: the starting value you choose or measure.

Output: the value that depends on the input.

Relationship: the way the input and output are connected.

Example 1

A bus pass costs \$4 to buy. Each ride costs \$3. The total cost is represented by $c=3r+4$.

Table

Rides, $r$0123
Cost, $c$471013

Graph

Explain how the context, equation, table, and graph each show the starting cost and the cost per ride.

🔴 Complete worked solution:
  • Context: The bus pass starts with a $\$4$ cost before any rides.
  • Equation: In $c=3r+4$, the $+4$ is the starting cost and the $3$ is the cost per ride.
  • Table: When $r=0$, $c=4$; then the cost increases by $3$ each ride.
  • Graph: The graph starts at $4$ on the cost axis and rises by $3$ for each ride.
🔴 Teacher Note: What students usually do wrong: they call $3$ the starting value because it appears first in the equation. Emphasize verbally: “The starting value is what happens when the input is $0$.”

You Try It 1

A gym charges a one-time membership fee of \$15 and then \$2 for each visit.

Write an equation for the total cost, make a quick table for $0$, $1$, $2$, and $3$ visits, and explain where the starting fee and cost per visit would appear on a graph.

🔴 Answer:
  • Let $v$ represent visits and $c$ represent total cost.
  • Equation: $c=2v+15$.
  • Table: $(0,15)$, $(1,17)$, $(2,19)$, $(3,21)$.
  • Graph: The starting fee appears at $15$ when visits are $0$; the cost increases by $2$ for each visit.
🔴 Teacher Note: Common misconception: writing $c=15v+2$. Quick CFU: “Which number happens once, and which number repeats each visit?”

Stop and Discuss

  1. Where do you see the 4-dollar starting cost in the table, graph, equation, and context?
  2. Where do you see the 3-dollar-per-ride change in the table, graph, equation, and context?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • The $4$ shows as the bus pass cost, the $+4$ in the equation, the table value when $r=0$, and the graph's starting point.
  • The $3$ shows as each ride cost, the coefficient of $r$, the table increasing by $3$, and the graph's steady rise.
🔴 Teacher Note: Connect to future slope/intercept language without requiring formal terminology yet.

Matching by What Changes

Two representations can match even if they look very different. Look for the same input-output pairs, the same starting value, and the same change.

Example 2

Which graph matches the table? Use evidence from the table and the graphs.

$x$01234
$y$13579

Write a sentence that uses at least two table values as evidence.

🔴 Complete worked solution:
  • Graph A matches the table.
  • Evidence: The table has $(0,1)$ and Graph A starts at $y=1$ when $x=0$.
  • Evidence: The table increases by $2$ each time $x$ increases by $1$, and Graph A shows that same upward pattern.
  • Graph B does not match because it decreases and starts near $8$.
🔴 Teacher Note: Expected observation: students should check at least two points or a point plus the rate of change. Common graph-reading error: choosing the graph that “looks close” without verifying coordinates.

You Try It 2

A different table starts with $y=8$ when $x=0$ and then decreases by $1$ each time $x$ increases by $1$.

Which graph above would match that table? Explain using at least two pieces of evidence.

🔴 Answer:
  • Graph B matches the new table.
  • Evidence: It starts at $y=8$ when $x=0$.
  • Evidence: It decreases by $1$ each time $x$ increases by $1$.
🔴 Teacher Note: Extension: Ask students to write three ordered pairs from Graph B before explaining.

Stop and Discuss

  1. What evidence should you check first when matching a table to a graph?
  2. Why is one point usually not enough evidence to prove a match?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • Check the starting value, several ordered pairs, and how the output changes.
  • One point is not enough because two different relationships can share a point but change differently.
🔴 Teacher Note: Emphasize evidence: “Show me a point and show me the change.”

Reading Each Representation for Meaning

A representation is more useful when we can explain what the numbers mean. We should connect numbers to the situation, not just copy them.

Example 3

A water tank already has $12$ gallons in it. Water is added at a steady rate of $4$ gallons each minute.

  1. Choose the equation that matches: $w=12m+4$ or $w=4m+12$.
  2. Explain what the input represents.
  3. Explain what the output represents.
  4. Describe how the table should change from one row to the next.
🔴 Complete worked solution:
  1. The matching equation is $w=4m+12$.
  2. The input $m$ represents minutes.
  3. The output $w$ represents gallons of water in the tank.
  4. The table should start at $12$ when $m=0$ and increase by $4$ gallons each minute.
🔴 Teacher Note: Key point: “already has” signals the starting value. What students usually do wrong: swap the $12$ and $4$ because the $12$ appears first in the sentence.

You Try It 3

A phone battery already has $30\%$ charge. It gains $5\%$ each minute while charging.

  1. Write an equation that matches the situation.
  2. Explain what the input and output represent.
  3. Describe how the table should change from one row to the next.
🔴 Answer:
  1. Equation: $b=5m+30$, where $m$ is minutes and $b$ is battery percent.
  2. Input: minutes charging. Output: battery percent.
  3. The table should start at $30$ when $m=0$ and increase by $5$ each minute.
🔴 Teacher Note: Common misconception: writing $b=30m+5$. Quick CFU: “What is true before any minutes pass?”

Stop and Discuss

  1. Why is $w=12m+4$ a tempting mistake?
  2. How can the words “already has” help you choose the equation?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • It is tempting because $12$ appears first in the story, but it is not the amount added each minute.
  • “Already has” means the starting amount, so it should be added at the end as the initial value.
🔴 Teacher Note: Connect to meaning before symbols: rate repeats; start happens once.

When Representations Do Not Match

Sometimes one representation has an error. To find it, compare the meaning, not just the numbers.

Example 4

A school club sells shirts. The club pays a \$20 setup fee and then pays \$6 for each shirt.

A student writes the equation $c=20s+6$.

  1. Explain the student’s mistake.
  2. Write the correct equation.
  3. Describe what a matching table would show when $s=0$, $s=1$, and $s=2$.
  4. Explain how a graph would show the setup fee.
🔴 Complete worked solution:
  1. The student switched the setup fee and the cost per shirt.
  2. Correct equation: $c=6s+20$.
  3. Matching table values: when $s=0$, $c=20$; when $s=1$, $c=26$; when $s=2$, $c=32$.
  4. A graph would show the setup fee by starting at $20$ on the cost axis when $s=0$.
🔴 Teacher Note: Modeling note: students should explain what each number means, not just move numbers around. Red flag: students say “because that is the formula” without connecting to setup fee and per-shirt cost.

You Try It 4

A fundraiser has a \$12 starting donation and earns \$4 for each bracelet sold. A student writes $d=12b+4$.

  1. Explain the mistake.
  2. Write the correct equation.
  3. Describe one table value and one graph feature that would prove your equation matches the situation.
🔴 Answer:
  1. The student switched the starting donation and the amount earned per bracelet.
  2. Correct equation: $d=4b+12$.
  3. Table evidence: when $b=0$, $d=12$; when $b=1$, $d=16$.
  4. Graph evidence: the graph starts at $12$ and increases by $4$ per bracelet.
🔴 Teacher Note: Quick CFU: “Which value should show up when zero bracelets are sold?” Extension: Ask students to create the incorrect table for $d=12b+4$ and explain why it does not match.

Stop and Discuss

  1. How can an error in an equation create an error in the table and graph?
  2. What evidence would help you catch the error before graphing?
🔴 Sample student responses:
  • If the equation is wrong, the outputs in the table and the points on the graph will also be wrong.
  • Check what happens at input $0$ and check what repeats each time the input increases by $1$.
🔴 Teacher Note: Connect to previous learning: matching representations means the meaning, not just the numbers, must agree.

Summary

  • A relationship connects two quantities.
  • Tables, graphs, equations, and contexts can show the same relationship.
  • A table shows input-output pairs.
  • A graph shows the pattern visually.
  • An equation gives a rule using symbols.
  • A context explains what the quantities mean in words.
  • Good justification uses evidence from more than one representation.

Common Mistakes

  • Matching by looks only: Two representations match because the relationship is the same, not because the pages look similar.
  • Mixing up input and output: Always ask which quantity is chosen or measured first and which quantity depends on it.
  • Ignoring the start value: The value when the input is $0$ can be important evidence.
  • Ignoring the change: Check how the output changes when the input increases.
  • Swapping numbers in an equation: In $y=mx+b$, the change rate and starting value have different meanings.
  • Not explaining evidence: A correct match is stronger when you can say why it matches.
