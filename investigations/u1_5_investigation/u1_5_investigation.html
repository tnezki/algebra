Algebra 1 - Unit 1

Section 1.5 Investigation

Equations as Models

Investigation: Model Toolbox: Which Form Answers the Question?

Time: 15-20 minutes

Format: Decision situation, representation comparison, exact equation model, and interpretation check

Learning Target: Write, solve, and interpret equations that model situations.
Investigation Directions

Setup

  • Grouping: Partners or groups of 3.
  • Materials: Student handout, pencil, and optional calculator.
  • Teacher display: Project the park situation on Page 3. Do not begin with an equation.
  • Timing: 3 minutes launch, 8-10 minutes building models, 4 minutes interpretation check, 3-5 minutes discussion.

Student Goal

Today you will investigate which representation is most useful for answering different questions about the same situation.

$\text{Words} \qquad \text{Table} \qquad \text{Graph} \qquad \text{Equation} \qquad \text{Interpretation}$

By the end, you should be able to explain why an equation is a useful model when we need an exact answer.

Norms for the Investigation

  • Choose a tool for a reason.
  • Explain what your representation helps you see.
  • Keep units attached to your answer.
  • A solution is not finished until it answers the original question.

Class Notes / Final Discovery
Teaching Resource 1: Teacher Script

Purpose

This investigation helps students discover why equations are useful models. The goal is not only to solve $8+3r=2+5r$, but to understand why that equation is the right tool for the exact equal-cost question.

Launch: 2-3 minutes

Say: “Today the question is not just can we solve an equation. The question is when is an equation the right tool?”

Display: Park A and Park B prices. Ask students to answer the 1-ride and 5-ride questions mentally or with quick arithmetic before writing equations.

Investigation Moves

  1. Choose Your Tool: Push students to name why a representation helps. Do not accept “because it is easy” without more detail.
  2. Table: Ask, “What specific values can the table check quickly?” and “When would the table become inefficient?”
  3. Graph: Ask, “What does the intersection mean?” and “How exact is our graph?”
  4. Equation: Ask, “Why are we setting these expressions equal instead of adding them?”
  5. Interpretation: Require units. The solution $r=3$ means 3 rides, not 3 dollars.

Closure / Synthesis

Final summary: We change representations because different representations answer different questions well. Tables help test values, graphs show trends and approximate intersections, equations give exact answers, and context gives the answer meaning.

Teaching Resource 2: Expected Observations and Misconceptions

Expected Student Observations

  • Park B starts cheaper because its admission cost is lower.
  • Park A increases more slowly because its per-ride cost is lower.
  • The table is useful for checking individual ride counts.
  • The graph makes the equal-cost point visible, but the equation confirms it exactly.
  • The solution must be interpreted in the context of rides and total cost.

Common Misconceptions to Watch For

  • Students may say $r=3$ means 3 dollars instead of 3 rides.
  • Students may write $8+3r+2+5r$ instead of setting the costs equal.
  • Students may forget to define the variable.
  • Students may think the graph is more exact than an algebraic solution.
  • Students may solve correctly but not answer the original decision question.

Discussion Prompts

  • Which tool was most useful first? Which tool became most useful later?
  • What changed when we moved from table to graph to equation?
  • What information would be lost if we only wrote $r=3$?

Part 6: Model Match / Reasonableness Check

Original task: Interpret $m=3$ for $12+4m=6+6m$.

Correct interpretation: After 3 meetings, the two clubs cost the same amount.

Why the other interpretation is wrong: The variable $m$ represents the number of meetings, not the cost per meeting. The number 3 is a meeting count, not a dollar amount.

Check: Club A: $12+4(3)=24$. Club B: $6+6(3)=24$.
