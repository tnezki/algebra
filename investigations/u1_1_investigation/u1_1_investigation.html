Algebra 1 - Unit 1

Section 1.1 Investigation

Multiple Representations of Relationships

Investigation: The Case of the Missing Representations

Time: 15-20 minutes

Format: Match-up, missing representation challenge, partner talk, and whole-class discussion

Learning Target: Connect tables, graphs, equations, and contexts that represent the same relationship.
Investigation Directions

Setup

  • Grouping: Partners or groups of 3.
  • Materials: Student handout, pencil, and scissors if students physically cut and sort cards.
  • Teacher display: Project one simple table and ask students what graph, equation, or story could match it.
  • Timing: 5 minutes for card sorting, 8-10 minutes for missing representation challenges, and 5 minutes for discussion and synthesis.

Student Goal

Today you will investigate how one relationship can be shown in different ways.

$\text{Context} \qquad \text{Table} \qquad \text{Graph} \qquad \text{Equation}$

By the end, you should be able to explain how you know when different representations describe the same relationship.

Norms for the Investigation

  • Match by meaning, not by surface features only.
  • Ask: “What quantities are changing?” and “How are they changing?”
  • Use at least two pieces of evidence before deciding that representations match.
  • Be ready to explain your reasoning to another group.

  4. How can you tell from a graph whether it matches a context?
  5. If one representation changes, what has to happen to the other representations?
Class Notes / Final Discovery
Teaching Resource 1: Teacher Script

Purpose

This investigation helps students discover that a relationship can be represented with a context, table, graph, and equation. The main conceptual goal is not formal function notation yet. Students should notice that each representation shows the same connection between changing quantities.

Launch: 2 minutes

Say: “Today you are solving a matching mystery. Each relationship can wear four different outfits: a story, a table, a graph, and an equation. Your job is to prove which ones belong together.”

Display: Show the table with $x$ values 0, 1, 2 and $y$ values 8, 10, 12. Ask: “What is changing? What stays consistent? What story could this describe?”

Investigation: 10-12 minutes

  1. Part 1: Let students match the complete relationships first. Ask, “What evidence proves these belong together?”
  2. Part 2: Move one challenge page at a time. Students should not need to see all missing representation tasks at once.
  3. Challenge D: Listen for “starts at 5” and “goes up by 2.”
  4. Challenge E: Push students to include a starting value of 10 and a change of 5 per input.
  5. Challenge F: Ask students to connect the $y$-intercept to the plant’s starting height.
  6. Challenge G: Allow different contexts as long as they match the graph’s starting value and repeated change.

Expected Student Observations

  • The value when $x=0$ appears in the table, graph, equation, and context.
  • The repeated change can be seen in table differences, graph steepness, and the number multiplying $x$.
  • A graph, table, equation, and context can look different but still describe the same relationship.

Common Misconceptions to Watch For

  • Students may match cards by similar numbers instead of checking the whole relationship.
  • Students may confuse the starting value with the repeated change.
  • Students may write a context that includes the right numbers but does not describe the equation correctly.
  • Students may think the graph is only the drawn points instead of the relationship those points represent.

Synthesis: 5 minutes

Final summary: A relationship can be represented by a context, table, graph, and equation. The representations match when they describe the same changing quantities and the same rule connecting those quantities.

Closure prompt: “Choose one relationship from today and explain how all four representations show the same idea.”

Part 3: Build the Rule
  1. The table shows the relationship by listing input-output pairs.
  2. The graph shows the relationship by plotting input-output pairs on a coordinate plane.
  3. The equation shows the relationship by giving a rule that connects the quantities.
  4. The context shows the relationship by explaining what the quantities mean in a situation.
  5. Two representations match when they have the same relationship or rule between the quantities.
Discussion Questions
  1. The starting value is often clearest in the context, table at $x=0$, graph intercept, or constant term.
  2. The repeated change is often clearest in table differences, graph steepness, or coefficient of $x$.
  3. Substitute table values into the equation or check the starting value and repeated change.
  4. Check whether the graph’s starting value and direction/rate match the story.
  5. The other representations must change so they still describe the same relationship.
