Section 1.4 Investigation

Expressions and Structure

Investigation: Expression Match-Up Lab

Question: Can different expressions tell the same story?

Time: 15-20 minutes

Format: Match-up lab, structure hunt, same-or-different debate, and whole-class synthesis

Learning Target: Interpret and rewrite algebraic expressions by identifying structure, meaning, and equivalent forms.
Investigation Directions

Setup

  • Grouping: Partners or groups of 3.
  • Materials: Student handout, pencil, and scissors if students physically cut and sort cards.
  • Teacher display: Project Page 3 before students cut cards.
  • Timing: 3 minutes for launch, 7 minutes for match-up lab, 5 minutes for same-or-different debate, and 5 minutes for discussion and synthesis.

Student Goal

Today you will investigate how expressions can look different but still represent the same quantity.

$\text{Context} \qquad \text{Grouped Form} \qquad \text{Expanded Form} \qquad \text{Model}$

By the end, you should be able to explain how structure can prove that expressions are equivalent.

Norms for the Investigation

  • Match by meaning, not by surface features only.
  • Use evidence from at least two representations.
  • Be ready to explain why expressions are equivalent or not equivalent.
  • Revise your thinking when a counterexample appears.

Proof Tools

Choose a Tool to Prove Your Decision

  • Explain the structure: Tell what each expression means.
  • Model: Use groups or an area model.

Class Notes / Final Discovery
Teaching Resource 1: Teacher Script

Purpose

This investigation helps students discover that expressions can be rewritten without changing value when the structure is preserved. The emphasis is not speed simplifying; it is interpreting why forms such as $4(x+3)$ and $4x+12$ represent the same quantity.

Launch: 2-3 minutes

Say: “These expressions use many of the same symbols. Before calculating, decide whether they really tell the same story.”

Display: $3(x+4)$, $3x+4$, and $3x+12$. Have students make a quick prediction, then ask for one reason on each side.

Do not say yet: “Distribute the 3.” Instead, ask what the 3 is multiplying.

Investigation: 10-12 minutes

  1. Part 1: Let groups cut and sort the context, expression, and model cards. Ask students to point to the part of the story that matches each symbol.
  2. Part 2: Use the structure table to slow down reasoning. Students should explain the outside coefficient, the group inside parentheses, and the expanded terms.
  3. Part 3: Move to the same-or-different debate. Require students to prove at least one equivalent pair and one non-equivalent pair.
  4. WODB: Accept more than one answer when the reasoning is valid. Highlight that $3x+4$ is the only expression not equivalent to $3(x+4)$, but other choices can be defended by form or order.

Teacher Moves

  • “What is being repeated?”
  • “Where do you see that group in the story?”
  • “What did the expanded form make visible?”
  • “Can you find a counterexample?”
  • “Can you rewrite one expression so it matches the other?”
Teaching Resource 2: Expected Observations and Misconceptions

Expected Student Observations

  • Parentheses show a group that is being repeated.
  • The outside coefficient tells how many copies of the group there are.
  • Expanded form separates the repeated parts into like terms.
  • Combining like terms changes the form but not the value.
  • Same numbers do not always mean same structure.

Common Misconceptions to Watch For

  • Students may think $3(x+4)=3x+4$ because they distribute only to the variable.
  • Students may think $5x+2=5(x+2)$ because the same numbers appear.
  • Students may combine unlike terms, such as treating $3x+4+2x$ as $9x$.
  • Students may believe one matching substitution value proves equivalence for all values.
  • Students may ignore context and match cards by numbers only.

Closure / Synthesis

Final summary: Equivalent expressions may look different, but they have the same value for every input because their structure represents the same quantity.

Closure prompt: “Choose one matched set from today and explain why the grouped form and expanded form are equivalent.”

Teaching Resource 5: Answer Key Continued
Which One Doesn't Belong?

Original task: Choose which expression does not belong from $3(x+4)$, $3x+12$, $12+3x$, and $3x+4$.

Most important mathematical answer: $3x+4$ does not belong because it is not equivalent to the other three expressions.

Explanation: $3(x+4)=3x+12$, and $3x+12=12+3x$ by the commutative property of addition. The expression $3x+4$ has a constant term of 4, not 12.

Alternate acceptable reasoning: A student could say $3(x+4)$ does not belong because it is the only expression in grouped form. A student could say $12+3x$ does not belong because it is the only expression with the constant first. Accept these only if the reasoning is clear, then return to the equivalence question.

Build the Big Idea
  1. Parentheses can show a group or quantity being treated as one unit.
  2. The distributive property lets us rewrite a grouped expression as an equivalent expanded expression.
  3. Like terms can be combined when they have the same variable part.
  4. Two expressions are equivalent when they have the same value for every input.
  5. A substitution check can show whether expressions match for a chosen input, but to prove equivalence we need structure, rewriting, or a general argument.
Discussion Questions
  1. $4(x+3)$ and $4x+12$ are equivalent because four groups of $x+3$ produce four $x$ terms and four groups of 3.
  2. $3(x+4)$ and $3x+4$ are not equivalent because the 3 must multiply the 4 as well as the $x$.
  3. A model helps show the repeated groups and why all parts must be counted.
  4. Substitution is useful for testing and finding counterexamples.
  5. Structure is more reliable because the same numbers can be arranged to mean different things.
