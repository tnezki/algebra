Algebra 1 - Unit 1: Foundations of Functions and Algebra

Essential Standard

Students will analyze relationships using functions and multiple representations in order to interpret patterns, communicate mathematical structure, and connect equations, tables, graphs, and contexts.
Section 1.1: Multiple Representations of Relationships

Learning Target: Students will connect tables, graphs, equations, and contexts that represent the same relationship.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify when different representations describe the same relationship.
  • I can match tables, graphs, equations, and situations.
  • I can describe how two quantities are related.
  • I can determine what quantities are changing.
  • I can justify how representations are connected.

Key Representations

  • Tables
  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Contextual situations
  • Verbal descriptions

Modeling Opportunities

  • cost vs quantity
  • distance vs time
  • earnings vs hours
  • matching cards across representations
Section 1.2: Inputs, Outputs, and Functions

Learning Target: Students will interpret functions as rules that connect inputs and outputs without relying on formal domain and range language.

I Can Statements

  • I can determine outputs from given inputs.
  • I can evaluate a function rule.
  • I can identify inputs and outputs in a situation.
  • I can describe how a function changes values.
  • I can decide whether a rule gives exactly one output for each input.

Key Representations

  • Function machines
  • Tables
  • Equations
  • Mappings
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • pricing rules
  • tax calculations
  • unit conversions
  • points or rewards systems
Section 1.3: Function Families

Learning Target: Students will recognize common function families from graphs, tables, equations, and contexts.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify common function families from graphs.
  • I can identify common function families from equations.
  • I can describe how function families behave.
  • I can compare different function families.
  • I can distinguish classification from full modeling.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Contexts
  • Parent function sketches

Modeling Opportunities

  • classification activities
  • matching graph shapes to equations
  • comparing growth patterns
Section 1.4: Expressions and Structure

Learning Target: Students will interpret and rewrite algebraic expressions by identifying structure, meaning, and equivalent forms.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify parts of an expression.
  • I can simplify expressions.
  • I can write equivalent expressions.
  • I can explain what an expression means in context.
  • I can use structure to choose an efficient rewrite.

Key Representations

  • Symbolic expressions
  • Verbal descriptions
  • Area models
  • Tables
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • geometry formulas
  • cost formulas
  • pattern rules
  • quantity relationships
Section 1.5: Equations as Models

Learning Target: Students will write, solve, and interpret equations that model situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can write equations to represent situations.
  • I can solve equations and interpret solutions.
  • I can determine whether a solution is reasonable.
  • I can explain what a solution means in context.
  • I can compare equation solving with other solution strategies.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Contexts
  • Verbal descriptions
  • Graphs

Modeling Opportunities

  • break-even situations
  • ticket sales
  • geometry contexts
  • proportional situations
Unit Modeling Themes
  • matching representations of the same relationship
  • cost and quantity situations
  • distance and time relationships
  • earnings and hours worked
  • function-rule situations
  • classification of relationship types
  • equations that model decisions

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, representation match, graph/equation identification, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, recognize representations, identify inputs and outputs, classify basic function-family shapes, and use notation correctly.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Representation Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students identify tables, graphs, equations, contexts, variables, and representation features.
  2. Input-Output Fluency Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students identify inputs and outputs and evaluate simple function rules.
  3. Function Family Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students recognize common function families from basic graphs and equations.
  4. Expression and Equation Notation Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify expression parts, equation structure, variables, and solutions.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need additional support with the language and notation of functions, representations, expressions, and equations before moving into deeper modeling and justification.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, mini-quiz, matching task, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can evaluate function rules, match representations, rewrite expressions, classify function families, and write equations from situations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Multiple Representation Match Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students match a table, graph, equation, and context that represent the same relationship.
  2. Function Rule Evaluation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students evaluate a function rule for several inputs and interpret the outputs.
  3. Function Family Sorting Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students sort graphs and equations into function families and explain simple features.
  4. Equation Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students write and solve an equation from a context and interpret the solution.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally use procedures and representations, but the assessment reveals whether they understand how relationships, rules, expression structure, and equations connect.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, representation analysis, strategy comparison, error analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify representation matches, analyze function behavior, explain expression structure, and defend the reasonableness of equations as models.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Representation Justification Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students justify whether multiple representations describe the same relationship using mathematical evidence.
  2. Function Behavior Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students compare two function families and explain differences in graph, table, and equation behavior.
  3. Expression Structure Analysis Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students analyze equivalent expressions and explain why different forms reveal different information.
  4. Equation Model Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students critique an equation model or solution interpretation and revise the reasoning.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand functions and algebraic relationships as connected systems of representations rather than isolated procedures. These tasks identify reasoning gaps and readiness for later linear modeling.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer function and algebra foundations to unfamiliar situations, create coherent models, and justify conclusions across multiple representations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Build a Relationship Web Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students create a table, graph, equation, and context for the same relationship and justify the connections.
  2. Design a Function Rule Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students design a function rule for a situation, evaluate it, and explain the meaning of inputs and outputs.
  3. Function Family Classification Challenge Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students create examples and non-examples of function families and defend classifications.
  4. Equation as a Model Performance Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students create, solve, interpret, and critique an equation model for a real-world situation.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, connect, critique, and revise representations and models. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 1 Summative Assessment Unit 1 Assessed During Unit 3

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of representation tasks, function-rule tasks, expression and equation tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize multiple representations, function notation and input-output reasoning, function-family recognition, expression structure, equation modeling, representation connections, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 1 is assessed during Unit 3 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 1 concepts: functions, multiple representations, expression structure, and equations as models. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 1 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 1 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 1 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
