Algebra 1 - Unit 0A: Algebra Foundations Review

Essential Standard

Students will analyze, represent, solve, model, interpret, and justify foundational algebra work involving rational number operations, expressions, equations, and inequalities in mathematical and contextual situations.

Section Outline

Section 0A.1: Rational Number Operations

Learning Target: Students will accurately perform operations with integers, fractions, and decimals while interpreting results in mathematical and contextual situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can construct accurate rational number computations using integers, fractions, and decimals in mathematical and contextual situations.
  • I can apply operation rules to calculate sums, differences, products, and quotients with rational numbers.
  • I can assess whether rational number results are reasonable for the situation they represent.

Key Representations

  • Integer expressions
  • Fraction operations
  • Decimal operations
  • Number line models
  • Contextual quantities

Modeling Opportunities

  • Model gains and losses with signed numbers
  • Represent shared quantities with fractions
  • Use decimals to represent measurements and money-like quantities
  • Check the reasonableness of results in context
Section 0A.2: Order of Operations and Evaluating Expressions

Learning Target: Students will evaluate numerical and algebraic expressions correctly using substitution and order of operations.

I Can Statements

  • I can formulate a clear sequence of steps for evaluating numerical and algebraic expressions.
  • I can use substitution and order of operations to calculate expression values accurately.
  • I can determine whether an expression value makes sense after substitution.

Key Representations

  • Numerical expressions
  • Algebraic expressions
  • Substitution tables
  • Operation hierarchy
  • Contextual expression values

Modeling Opportunities

  • Represent repeated calculation steps with expressions
  • Connect variable values to expression outputs
  • Use grouping symbols to model intended calculation order
  • Interpret evaluated results in context
Section 0A.3: Expressions and Properties

Learning Target: Students will analyze and rewrite algebraic expressions using properties of operations and algebraic structure.

I Can Statements

  • I can build equivalent algebraic expressions using properties of operations and structure.
  • I can extend expression work by rewriting terms, factors, and sums into useful equivalent forms.
  • I can critique whether two rewritten expressions preserve the same value for all valid inputs.

Key Representations

  • Equivalent expressions
  • Terms and coefficients
  • Factors and products
  • Distributive property structures
  • Algebra tiles or area models

Modeling Opportunities

  • Model repeated groups with the distributive property
  • Represent combined quantities with like terms
  • Use area models to connect products and sums
  • Revise expressions to reveal structure
Section 0A.4: Solving Equations

Learning Target: Students will solve one-variable equations and interpret solutions within mathematical and contextual situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can design a balanced equation-solving pathway that keeps both sides equivalent.
  • I can use inverse operations to solve one-variable equations accurately.
  • I can assess whether a solution satisfies the original equation and fits the context.

Key Representations

  • One-variable equations
  • Balance models
  • Inverse operations
  • Solution checks
  • Contextual equations

Modeling Opportunities

  • Model unknown quantities with variables
  • Represent equal quantities with equations
  • Use balance reasoning to maintain equivalence
  • Interpret solutions as answers to context questions
Section 0A.5: Solving and Graphing Inequalities

Learning Target: Students will solve inequalities, represent solution sets graphically, and interpret solutions in context.

I Can Statements

  • I can construct inequality models for situations with more than one possible solution.
  • I can apply inverse operations to solve inequalities and account for direction changes when needed.
  • I can extend solutions by representing and interpreting solution sets on a number line.

Key Representations

  • One-variable inequalities
  • Number line graphs
  • Open and closed circles
  • Solution set notation
  • Contextual constraints

Modeling Opportunities

  • Model constraints with inequality symbols
  • Represent multiple possible solutions on number lines
  • Connect inequality direction to contextual meaning
  • Interpret boundary values and feasible solutions
Unit Modeling Themes
  • Using rational numbers to represent quantities, changes, and comparisons
  • Connecting expressions to repeated calculations and contextual values
  • Rewriting algebraic structures to reveal meaning and efficiency
  • Solving equations as balanced models of unknown quantities
  • Representing constraints and solution sets with inequalities and graphs

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, representation recognition, or exit ticket

Purpose: DOK 1 reveals whether students have the prerequisite fluency and notation control needed for rational number operations, substitution, expressions, equations, and inequalities.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Rational Number Operations Check Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.1 - Compute with integers, fractions, and decimals; apply sign rules across all four operations; use absolute value to find distance between two numbers.
  2. Evaluate Expressions Check Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.2 - Substitute given values into algebraic expressions; apply order of operations including exponents and absolute value; recognize when an expression is undefined.
  3. Solve Linear Equations Check Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.4 - Solve one- and two-step equations; verify a proposed solution by substitution; classify equations as one solution, no solution, or all real numbers.
  4. Inequality Basics Check Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.5 - Solve one-step inequalities including cases requiring sign reversal; identify open or closed boundary point and shade direction; graph a solution set on a number line.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 1 performance shows whether students can control signs across all four operations, substitute into expressions correctly, solve basic equations, and graph inequality solution sets before more complex algebraic reasoning begins.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Mini-quiz, graph/table task, matching task, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: DOK 2 reveals whether students can select and carry out procedures across connected algebra foundations rather than completing isolated one-step tasks only.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Rational Number Contexts Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.1 - Model multi-step change scenarios with rational number expressions; scale quantities using multiplication and division; evaluate a mixed fraction-decimal expression with a sign-reasonableness check.
  2. Expression Meaning Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.3 - Evaluate and interpret linear model expressions in context; contrast two structurally different expressions to explain the difference in meaning; complete a function table and identify the minimum output.
  3. Equation Contexts Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.4 - Write and solve an equation from a perimeter context; solve multi-step equations using distribution and check by substitution; create a two-step equation with a specified solution and verify.
  4. Solve and Graph Inequalities Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.5 - Solve two-step inequalities and graph solution sets; solve inequalities with negative coefficients and explain direction changes; write and solve contextual inequalities; graph compound inequalities with boundary tests.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 2 performance shows whether students can apply rational number operations and algebraic procedures in context, interpret expression structure and meaning, and connect equation and inequality solving to number line representations.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, model critique, strategy comparison, representation analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: DOK 3 reveals whether students can reason about why algebraic steps work, when results are reasonable, and how representations preserve meaning.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Rational Number Reasoning Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.1 - Critique incorrect fraction operation claims and supply the corrected result; compute a total change using both fraction and decimal form and confirm the methods agree; construct a step-by-step strategy for evaluating a complex rational expression.
  2. Expression Structure Analysis Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.2 - Identify and correct evaluation errors caused by misapplying exponents or sign rules; analyze why operation order within an expression determines its value; match an expression to the context it correctly models.
  3. Equivalent Expressions Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.3 - Construct a strategy for verifying equivalence across all inputs rather than one test value; analyze what factored and expanded forms each reveal about structure; simplify an expression two ways and explain why the results agree.
  4. Inequality Reasoning Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.5 - Construct a strategy for solving and graphing a multi-step inequality addressing distribution, negative division, and boundary type; solve an inequality two ways and compare; analyze a student's boundary value claim and revise if needed.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 3 performance shows whether students can critique flawed reasoning, construct solution strategies, verify equivalence beyond a single test case, and reason about why boundary values and operation order determine the meaning of algebraic results.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or capstone synthesis task

Purpose: DOK 4 reveals whether students can synthesize foundational algebra skills into compact models that connect operations, expressions, equations, inequalities, and context.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Rational Number Models Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.1 - Design a multi-entry change log with a required net change using at least one fraction, decimal, and integer; identify and correct a fraction-to-decimal conversion error in a given model; connect absolute value distance and signed addition as two representations of the same quantity.
  2. Expression Design Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.3 - Create an expression with specified structural features that evaluates to a target output; design a real-world context for a given expression and interpret the result; write a context where two structurally different expressions represent meaningfully different situations.
  3. Equation Design Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.4 - Create a one-variable equation requiring distribution with a specified solution and verify by substitution; write a real-world context for a given equation and interpret the solution; synthesize a table, equation, and written sentence for a situation.
  4. Inequality Models Can Be Assessed After Section 0A.5 - Design a real-world context for a given two-step inequality and describe feasible values; compare two student inequality models for the same situation and recommend the stronger model with justification; create a compound inequality with a minimum and maximum constraint and justify the bounds.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design original models, connect rational number operations, expression structure, equation solving, and inequality reasoning to real contexts. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 0A Summative Assessment Beginning-of-course checkpoint; revisited throughout later units

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of rational number computation, expression evaluation, expression rewriting, equation solving, inequality solving, number line graphing, and contextual interpretation tasks aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize accuracy with integer, fraction, and decimal operations; correct use of substitution and order of operations; algebraic structure and equivalent forms; balanced equation solving; inequality solution sets; and interpretation of results in mathematical and contextual situations.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 0A content is revisited through subsequent units via retrieval practice, warm-ups, and formative assessments.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates strong flexible understanding of rational number operations, expression structure, equation solving, inequality graphing, and contextual interpretation. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential algebra foundation concepts in rational number operations, expressions, equations, and inequalities. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of foundational algebra concepts and procedures. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of rational number operations, expression structure, equation solving, inequality graphing, or contextual interpretation. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
