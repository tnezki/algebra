Algebra 1 - Unit 7: Exponential Functions and Modeling

Essential Standard

Students will analyze, represent, and model exponential relationships in order to interpret growth and decay, compare function behavior, and make predictions using equations, graphs, tables, and contexts.
Section 7.1: Recognizing Growth and Decay

Learning Target: Students will identify exponential relationships from patterns, tables, graphs, and situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can recognize exponential patterns.
  • I can distinguish exponential and linear growth.
  • I can identify growth and decay situations.
  • I can describe how exponential relationships change over time.
  • I can identify repeated multiplication in tables and contexts.

Key Representations

  • Tables
  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Contexts
  • Sequences

Modeling Opportunities

  • populations
  • bacteria growth
  • social media growth
  • depreciation
  • doubling and halving situations

Learning Target: Students will write exponential equations from tables, graphs, and situations.

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, table-pattern check, graph recognition, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize exponential patterns, identify growth and decay, use vocabulary, and name initial values, multipliers, growth factors, and decay factors.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Growth and Decay Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students classify patterns and situations as growth, decay, or not exponential.
  2. Multiplier and Initial Value Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students identify initial values, multipliers, and percent changes from tables and equations.
  3. Exponential Graph Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students recognize exponential graphs and distinguish growth from decay.
  4. Function Family Sorting Check Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students sort linear, quadratic, and exponential representations.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need additional support seeing repeated multiplication and connecting exponential vocabulary to tables, graphs, and contexts.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, mini-quiz, graph/table task, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can write exponential equations, graph exponential relationships, calculate values, and make routine predictions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Write an Exponential Equation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students write an equation from a table, graph, or percent-change context.
  2. Graph and Interpret an Exponential Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students graph an exponential function and interpret growth or decay.
  3. Compare Function Families Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students compare linear, quadratic, and exponential tables or graphs.
  4. Exponential Prediction Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students use an exponential model to make and interpret a prediction.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally use exponential procedures and representations, but the assessment reveals whether they connect the calculations to meaning.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, model critique, strategy comparison, representation analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify exponential models, explain growth and decay behavior, compare function families, and critique unreasonable predictions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Repeated Multiplication Justification Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students explain why a relationship is exponential using tables, equations, or contexts.
  2. Parameter Interpretation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students justify the meaning of initial value and multiplier in context.
  3. Linear vs Exponential Argument Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students compare representations and defend a model choice.
  4. Prediction Reasonableness Critique Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students critique a prediction made from an exponential model.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand exponential functions as connected models rather than just formulas for repeated calculation.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer exponential reasoning to unfamiliar situations, create models, compare model families, and justify decisions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Growth or Decay Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students design an exponential situation and equation, then interpret parameters.
  2. Model Selection Challenge Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students choose between linear, quadratic, and exponential models and justify the choice.
  3. Exponential Data Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students create a model from data and communicate a prediction with limitations.
  4. Critique and Revise a Prediction Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students critique an exponential prediction and revise the conclusion.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, critique, compare, and make decisions with exponential models. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 7 Summative Assessment Unit 7 Assessed During Future Course / Final Exam

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of exponential recognition tasks, equation-writing tasks, graph and table interpretation tasks, function-family comparison tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize growth and decay, initial values, multipliers, percent change, exponential equations, graph behavior, model comparison, prediction, representation connections, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 7 is assessed during a future course checkpoint or final exam after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 7 concepts: exponential growth and decay, exponential equations, graph behavior, function-family comparison, and exponential modeling. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 7 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 7 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 7 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
