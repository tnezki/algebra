Algebra 1 – Unit 0A: Algebra Foundations Review

Essential Standard

Students will demonstrate fluency with numerical reasoning, algebraic expressions, equations, and inequalities in order to support future function analysis, modeling, and algebraic problem solving.
Section 0A.1: Rational Number Operations

Learning Target: Students will accurately perform operations with integers, fractions, and decimals while interpreting results in mathematical and contextual situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can add and subtract rational numbers.
  • I can multiply and divide rational numbers.
  • I can evaluate numerical expressions involving rational numbers.
  • I can apply rational number operations in real-world contexts.

Key Representations

  • Number lines
  • Numerical expressions
  • Contextual situations
Section 0A.2: Order of Operations and Evaluating Expressions

Learning Target: Students will evaluate numerical and algebraic expressions correctly using substitution and order of operations.

I Can Statements

  • I can apply order of operations correctly.
  • I can evaluate expressions using substitution.
  • I can evaluate expressions containing exponents.
  • I can interpret expressions in context.

Key Representations

  • Numerical expressions
  • Algebraic expressions
  • Tables
  • Contexts
Section 0A.3: Expressions and Properties

Learning Target: Students will analyze and rewrite algebraic expressions using properties of operations and algebraic structure.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify terms, coefficients, and variables.
  • I can combine like terms.
  • I can apply the distributive property.
  • I can write equivalent expressions.
  • I can explain how structure can make an expression easier to interpret.

Key Representations

  • Symbolic expressions
  • Area models
  • Verbal descriptions
Section 0A.4: Solving Equations

Learning Target: Students will solve one-variable equations and interpret solutions within mathematical and contextual situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can solve one-step equations.
  • I can solve multi-step equations.
  • I can verify solutions.
  • I can write equations from situations.
  • I can interpret solutions in context.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • Geometry formulas
  • Cost situations
  • Quantity relationships
Section 0A.5: Solving and Graphing Inequalities

Learning Target: Students will solve inequalities, represent solution sets graphically, and interpret solutions in context.

I Can Statements

  • I can solve one-variable inequalities.
  • I can graph solution sets on a number line.
  • I can determine whether a value satisfies an inequality.
  • I can interpret inequality solutions in context.
  • I can compare equation and inequality solutions.

Key Representations

  • Inequalities
  • Number lines
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • Minimum and maximum requirements
  • Real-world restrictions

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, quick check, proficiency check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, perform rational number operations, use algebraic notation correctly, and demonstrate foundational numerical fluency.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Rational Number Fluency Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students perform operations with integers, fractions, and decimals.
  2. Number Sense Retrieval Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 – Students estimate, compare, and evaluate numerical expressions.
  3. Order of Operations Quick Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students evaluate expressions involving multiple operations and exponents.
  4. Vocabulary and Notation Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students identify variables, coefficients, terms, expressions, equations, and inequalities.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need additional fluency support before deeper algebraic reasoning can occur. Difficulties often reveal gaps in prerequisite arithmetic or mathematical vocabulary.
DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4

Format: Exit ticket, whiteboard response, partner check, short written task, or mini-quiz

Purpose: Check whether students can evaluate expressions, simplify algebraic expressions, apply properties, and solve routine equations and inequalities.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Expression Evaluation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 – Students evaluate expressions using substitution and order of operations.
  2. Equivalent Expression Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students simplify expressions and justify why expressions are equivalent.
  3. Equation Solving Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students solve one-step and multi-step equations and verify solutions.
  4. Inequality Graphing Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students solve inequalities and represent solution sets on number lines.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally carry out procedures and use appropriate algebraic tools. These assessments reveal whether students understand how procedures connect to algebraic structure and representation.
DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6

Format: Constructed response, discourse prompt, strategy comparison, error analysis, or written explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can explain reasoning, justify procedures, analyze mathematical structure, and critique mathematical arguments.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Strategy Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students solve an equation using two approaches and compare efficiency and clarity.
  2. Error Analysis Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students critique incorrect work involving equations or inequalities and explain the misconception.
  3. Structure and Properties Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 – Students explain how properties of operations justify simplification steps.
  4. Solution Justification Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students defend whether a proposed solution is valid and support their reasoning with mathematical evidence.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students truly understand why procedures work rather than simply following steps. These tasks identify misconceptions, reasoning gaps, and readiness for future modeling work.
DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8

Format: Performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer foundational algebra skills to unfamiliar situations, model real-world contexts, and justify conclusions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Budget Constraint Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students create and interpret inequalities to model spending constraints and justify recommendations.
  2. Design an Equation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 – Students create a real-world situation represented by a given equation and explain the meaning of the solution.
  3. Critique and Revise Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students evaluate flawed mathematical reasoning, identify errors, and rewrite a valid solution process.
  4. Multi-Representation Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 – Students analyze a situation using numerical reasoning, expressions, equations, and inequalities, then communicate conclusions using mathematical evidence.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can transfer foundational algebra skills beyond routine exercises. These assessments emphasize reasoning, communication, modeling, and decision making rather than procedural execution alone.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 0A Summative Assessment Unit 0A Assessed During Unit 1

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of procedural fluency, application tasks, representation tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize numerical reasoning, algebraic fluency, expression analysis, equation solving, inequality solving, representation skills, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1–3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 0A is assessed during Unit 1 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A — Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 0A concepts: numerical reasoning, expressions, equations, and inequalities. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently applies algebraic structure, verifies solutions appropriately, communicates mathematically with precision, and connects procedures to meaning and context.

B — Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 0A concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, and the student demonstrates appropriate use of algebraic procedures, notation, and representations.

C — Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 0A concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with multi-step procedures, representations, algebraic structure, or mathematical communication. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is sufficient evidence that the student understands the major concepts.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 0A concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak numerical fluency, misunderstanding of algebraic structure, or difficulty solving and interpreting equations and inequalities. Additional instruction, practice, and reassessment may be needed before proficiency can be demonstrated.
