Algebra 1 - Unit 2: Linear Functions and Modeling

Essential Standard

Students will analyze, represent, and model linear relationships in order to interpret constant rate of change, make predictions, and connect equations, tables, graphs, and contexts.
Section 2.1: Rate of Change and Slope

Learning Target: Students will interpret slope as a measure of constant rate of change.

I Can Statements

  • I can calculate slope from graphs.
  • I can calculate slope from tables.
  • I can interpret slope in context.
  • I can determine whether a relationship has a constant rate of change.
  • I can connect slope to unit rate and change.

Key Representations

  • Tables
  • Graphs
  • Contexts
  • Slope triangles
  • Ordered pairs

Modeling Opportunities

  • earnings
  • fuel consumption

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, graph feature check, slope/intercept identification, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize linear representations, identify slope and intercepts, read graphs and tables, and use linear modeling vocabulary correctly.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Slope and Intercept Identification Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students identify slope, intercepts, and direction of change from equations and graphs.
  2. Linear Representation Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students identify graphs, tables, equations, contexts, scatterplots, and lines of fit.
  3. Rate of Change Fluency Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students calculate simple rates of change from tables and ordered pairs.
  4. Scatterplot and Association Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify association, trends, and basic linear-model features from data displays.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need additional support with graph reading, coordinate reasoning, slope vocabulary, intercept meaning, and the basic visual language of linear functions.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, graphing task, mini-quiz, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can calculate slope, graph linear functions, write equations from representations, compare linear relationships, and use linear models for routine predictions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Slope from Multiple Representations Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students find and interpret slope from a graph, table, and situation.
  2. Graphing Linear Functions Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students graph linear functions using slope and intercepts or tables.
  3. Writing Linear Equations Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students write linear equations from graphs, tables, and contexts.
  4. Linear Model Prediction Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students use a linear model or line of fit to make and interpret predictions.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally carry out procedures and connect representations, but the assessments reveal whether they understand slope and intercepts as meaningful quantities rather than isolated numbers.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, representation analysis, strategy comparison, error analysis, data-model critique, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify linear representation matches, interpret parameters in context, critique incorrect reasoning, and defend predictions using mathematical evidence.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Slope Interpretation Justification Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students explain and justify the meaning of slope in graphs, tables, and contexts.
  2. Linear Representation Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students compare two linear relationships and defend a conclusion using multiple representations.
  3. Equation Model Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students critique and revise a linear equation model or interpretation.
  4. Data Model Reasoning Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students analyze scatterplots, lines of fit, predictions, and residuals using contextual reasoning.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand linear functions as connected representations of constant change and whether they can communicate reasoning clearly enough to support future systems and modeling work.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer linear modeling to unfamiliar situations, design and compare models, critique assumptions, and justify decisions using equations, graphs, tables, and data.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Create a Linear Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students design a context, table, graph, and equation for the same linear relationship.
  2. Compare and Recommend Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students compare competing linear options and justify a recommendation.
  3. Data Modeling Performance Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students create or revise a linear model from data and explain limitations.
  4. Critique a Linear Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify flaws in a linear model, revise it, and justify the improved model.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, connect, critique, and revise linear models. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 2 Summative Assessment Unit 2 Assessed During Unit 4

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of slope and rate-of-change tasks, graphing tasks, equation-writing tasks, representation comparison tasks, data-modeling tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize rate of change, slope, intercepts, graphing linear functions, writing linear equations, comparing linear representations, scatterplots, lines of fit, prediction, residuals, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 2 is assessed during Unit 4 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 2 concepts: linear relationships, slope, graphing, equation writing, representation comparison, and linear modeling with data. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 2 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 2 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 2 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
