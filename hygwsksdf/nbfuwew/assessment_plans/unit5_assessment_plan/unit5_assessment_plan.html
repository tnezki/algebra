Algebra 1 - Unit 5: Factoring and Quadratic Functions

Essential Standard

Students will analyze, represent, factor, and solve quadratic relationships in order to connect algebraic structure, graphical behavior, and real-world applications.
Section 5.1: GCF and Structure

Learning Target: Students will identify common factors and use structure to rewrite polynomial expressions.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify common factors.
  • I can factor out a greatest common factor.
  • I can rewrite expressions in equivalent forms.
  • I can use structure to simplify expressions.
  • I can connect factored and expanded forms to area models.

Key Representations

  • Expressions
  • Area models
  • Generic rectangles
  • Factored forms
  • Verbal descriptions

Modeling Opportunities

  • area models
  • dimensions
  • geometric relationships
Section 5.2: Factoring Trinomials

Learning Target: Students will factor quadratic trinomials using patterns, products, sums, and structure.

I Can Statements

  • I can factor quadratic trinomials.
  • I can verify factors by multiplication.
  • I can use area models to factor.
  • I can recognize factor patterns.
  • I can explain how product and sum relationships support factoring.

Key Representations

  • Symbolic expressions
  • Area models
  • Generic rectangles
  • Product-sum diagrams
  • Tables of factor pairs

Modeling Opportunities

  • rectangle dimensions
  • area models
  • product-and-sum reasoning
Section 5.3: Special Factoring Patterns

Learning Target: Students will recognize and factor special polynomial patterns.

I Can Statements

  • I can recognize special factoring patterns.
  • I can factor perfect square trinomials.
  • I can factor differences of squares.
  • I can explain why special patterns work.
  • I can decide when a special pattern does not apply.

Key Representations

  • Symbolic expressions
  • Geometric models
  • Area models
  • Equivalent forms
  • Pattern tables

Modeling Opportunities

  • area models
  • square dimensions
  • geometric explanations of algebraic patterns
Section 5.4: Factored Form and Zeros

Learning Target: Students will connect factors, zeros, intercepts, and graphs of quadratic functions.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify zeros from factored form.
  • I can identify x-intercepts from a graph.
  • I can connect factors and solutions.
  • I can explain how zeros appear in multiple representations.
  • I can sketch or interpret a quadratic using zeros and other key features.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Factored form
  • Intercepts

Modeling Opportunities

  • projectile motion previews
  • revenue models
  • area models
  • zero-product contexts
Section 5.5: Comparing Quadratic Forms

Learning Target: Students will compare standard, vertex, and factored forms of quadratic functions and determine which form best reveals a given feature.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify key features from standard form.
  • I can identify key features from vertex form.
  • I can identify key features from factored form.
  • I can determine which form is most useful for a given situation.
  • I can justify why one form is more efficient than another for a specific question.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Contexts
  • Multiple algebraic forms

Modeling Opportunities

  • projectile motion
  • area optimization
  • revenue
  • feature-based decision making
Unit Modeling Themes
  • rectangle area and dimension models
  • quadratic graph features
  • zeros and break-even points
  • projectile-motion previews
  • revenue and profit models
  • choosing useful algebraic forms
  • connecting algebraic structure to graphical behavior

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, factor recognition, graph-feature check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, recognize factoring structures, identify quadratic graph features, and distinguish forms of quadratic expressions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. GCF and Factor Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students identify common factors, products, sums, terms, and factored forms.
  2. Trinomial Pattern Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students recognize factor pairs, trinomial structure, and area-model parts.
  3. Special Factoring Pattern Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students identify perfect square trinomials and differences of squares.
  4. Zeros and Quadratic Feature Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify zeros, intercepts, vertices, and useful forms from equations and graphs.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need support with the language of factors, zeros, and quadratic features before deeper reasoning about forms and graph behavior can occur.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, mini-quiz, area-model task, graph/equation match, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can factor expressions, use area models, verify factors, identify zeros from factored form, and select useful forms for routine questions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. GCF and Structure Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students factor common factors and connect expanded and factored forms.
  2. Trinomial Factoring Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students factor trinomials using product-sum reasoning or area models.
  3. Special Pattern Factoring Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students factor special patterns and verify by multiplication.
  4. Factored Form and Zeros Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students identify zeros and connect them to x-intercepts and graphs.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally carry out factoring procedures and connect them to representations, but the assessment reveals whether they understand the structure behind the procedure.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, representation analysis, strategy comparison, error analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify factoring methods, analyze errors, connect algebraic forms to graph features, and defend which form is most useful.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Factoring Strategy Defense Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students compare factoring methods and justify an efficient strategy.
  2. Special Pattern Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students explain why a special pattern works or why it does not apply.
  3. Zeros and Graph Connection Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students justify how factored form, zeros, and x-intercepts represent the same information.
  4. Quadratic Form Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students compare standard, vertex, and factored form and defend which is most useful for a given question.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand factoring and quadratic forms as connected representations rather than isolated symbolic moves.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer factoring and quadratic-form reasoning to unfamiliar situations, create models, and justify conclusions using multiple representations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Factoring Challenge Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students create a trinomial with intended factors and justify the construction.
  2. Special Pattern Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students critique and revise a flawed special-pattern argument.
  3. Zeros and Context Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students create a context where zeros or intercepts have meaning and explain limitations.
  4. Choose the Best Quadratic Form Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students select and defend the most useful form for answering different modeling questions.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, connect, critique, and revise quadratic models using algebraic structure and graph features. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 5 Summative Assessment Unit 5 Assessed During Unit 7

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of factoring tasks, graph-feature tasks, representation-connection tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize algebraic structure, greatest common factors, trinomial factoring, special factoring patterns, factored form, zeros, x-intercepts, quadratic graph features, comparing forms, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 5 is assessed during Unit 7 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 5 concepts: factoring, quadratic structure, zeros, graph features, and comparing quadratic forms. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 5 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 5 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 5 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
