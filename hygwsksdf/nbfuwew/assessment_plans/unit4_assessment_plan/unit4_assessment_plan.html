Algebra 1 - Unit 4: Function Families and Transformations

Essential Standard

Students will analyze function families and transformations in order to identify key features, connect equations and graphs, and describe how parameters affect function behavior.
Section 4.1: Recognizing Function Families

Learning Target: Students will identify common function families from graphs, equations, tables, and contexts.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify common function families from graphs.
  • I can identify common function families from equations.
  • I can describe how different function families behave.
  • I can compare function families using multiple representations.
  • I can distinguish classification from full modeling.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Contexts
  • Parent function sketches

Modeling Opportunities

  • classification activities
  • matching graph shapes to equations
  • comparing growth patterns
Section 4.2: Translations

Learning Target: Students will graph and write translated functions using key points and key features.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify horizontal shifts.
  • I can identify vertical shifts.
  • I can graph translated functions.
  • I can write equations from translated graphs.
  • I can describe how translations preserve or change key features.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Key points
  • Parent functions

Modeling Opportunities

  • projectile shifts
  • transformed paths
  • shifted growth situations
Section 4.3: Transformations

Learning Target: Students will analyze and graph transformations involving stretches, compressions, and reflections.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify reflections.
  • I can identify stretches and compressions.
  • I can graph transformed functions.
  • I can write equations from transformed graphs.
  • I can explain how a parameter changes graph behavior.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Transformation rules
  • Parent functions

Modeling Opportunities

  • graphical comparisons
  • parameter investigations
  • function-family sorting with transformations
Section 4.4: Polynomial Operations

Learning Target: Students will add, subtract, and multiply polynomial expressions.

I Can Statements

  • I can classify polynomials.
  • I can add and subtract polynomials.
  • I can multiply polynomials.
  • I can use structure to simplify polynomial expressions.
  • I can connect polynomial multiplication to area models.

Key Representations

  • Symbolic expressions
  • Area models
  • Generic rectangles
  • Tables
  • Verbal descriptions

Modeling Opportunities

  • area models
  • geometric contexts
  • rectangle dimensions
Section 4.5: Polynomial Structure and Quadratic Graphs

Learning Target: Students will connect polynomial expressions to quadratic graphs and key features.

I Can Statements

  • I can recognize quadratic expressions.
  • I can connect quadratic equations and graphs.
  • I can identify key features of quadratic graphs.
  • I can explain how polynomial structure affects graph behavior.
  • I can preview how factors and structure connect to zeros and intercepts.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Graphs
  • Tables
  • Area models
  • Quadratic features

Modeling Opportunities

  • projectile motion previews
  • area models
  • maximum and minimum situations
Unit Modeling Themes
  • recognizing families from multiple representations
  • describing translations and transformations
  • using parameters to predict graph behavior
  • polynomial area models and geometric structure
  • connecting quadratic expressions to graph features
  • previewing intercepts, vertices, and symmetry

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, graph/equation identification, feature check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize parent functions, transformation vocabulary, polynomial vocabulary, and basic quadratic graph features.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Function Family Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students identify common function families from graphs, equations, and tables.
  2. Translation Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students identify horizontal shifts, vertical shifts, parent functions, and key points.
  3. Transformation Feature Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students recognize reflections, stretches, compressions, and transformed graph features.
  4. Polynomial and Quadratic Feature Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify polynomial terms, degree, area-model parts, and basic quadratic graph features.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need more support with vocabulary, notation, and visual recognition before deeper transformation and polynomial reasoning can occur.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, graphing task, matching task, mini-quiz, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can classify families, graph translations, identify transformation effects, perform polynomial operations, and connect quadratic equations to graphs.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Function Family Sorting Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students sort graphs, equations, and tables by function family.
  2. Translation Graphing Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students graph translated functions and identify key point changes.
  3. Transformation Matching Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students match equations, descriptions, and graphs for reflections and vertical changes.
  4. Polynomial Area Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students add, subtract, and multiply polynomials using symbolic work and area models.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally apply procedures and connect representations, but the assessment reveals whether they understand how equations, graphs, and structure correspond.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, representation analysis, strategy comparison, error analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify classifications, explain transformation effects, analyze polynomial structure, and connect equivalent expressions to graph behavior.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Function Family Evidence Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students justify classifications using graph, table, and equation evidence.
  2. Transformation Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students explain how equation changes affect graph behavior and key features.
  3. Polynomial Structure Analysis Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students analyze equivalent polynomial forms and explain why operations preserve value.
  4. Quadratic Feature Connection Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students connect quadratic expressions, graphs, and visible features using mathematical evidence.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students can reason about transformations and structure rather than only identify shapes or perform symbolic operations.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer function-family, transformation, and polynomial-structure reasoning to unfamiliar situations and justify model choices.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Function Family Challenge Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students create examples and non-examples of function families and defend classifications.
  2. Transformation Design Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students design a transformed graph and equation that meet specific conditions.
  3. Polynomial Model Design Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students create a geometric or contextual model represented by polynomial expressions.
  4. Quadratic Structure Performance Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students connect polynomial structure to quadratic graph features and justify conclusions.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, connect, critique, and revise models using transformations and polynomial structure. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 4 Summative Assessment Unit 4 Assessed During Unit 6

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of function-family identification tasks, transformation tasks, polynomial operation tasks, quadratic-graph connection tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize function-family recognition, graph and equation connections, translations, transformations, polynomial operations, area models, quadratic graph features, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 4 is assessed during Unit 6 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 4 concepts: function families, transformations, polynomial operations, and quadratic graph structure. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 4 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 4 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 4 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
