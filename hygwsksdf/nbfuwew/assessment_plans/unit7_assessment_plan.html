Algebra 1 - Unit 7: Exponential Functions and Modeling

Essential Standard

Students will analyze, represent, model, and interpret exponential relationships using patterns, tables, graphs, equations, and real-world situations.

Section Outline

Section 7.1: Recognizing Growth and Decay

Learning Target: Students will identify exponential relationships from patterns, tables, graphs, and situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can construct evidence from patterns, tables, graphs, and situations to support an exponential growth or decay relationship.
  • I can apply repeated multiplication and constant ratios to exponential patterns.
  • I can determine whether a relationship is exponential by checking how values change across representations.

Key Representations

  • Tables with constant ratios
  • Graphs showing growth or decay curves
  • Verbal situations involving repeated percent change
  • Sequences of values or patterns
  • Equations in the form $y=ab^x$

Modeling Opportunities

  • Model population growth from a table of values
  • Model depreciation of an object over time
  • Connect repeated percent change to growth or decay factors
  • Sort evidence from tables, graphs, and contexts by type of change
Section 7.2: Writing Exponential Equations

Learning Target: Students will write exponential equations from tables, graphs, and situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can build exponential equations using an initial value and a growth or decay factor.
  • I can use exponential equations to predict values from tables, graphs, and situations.
  • I can formulate exponential equations that represent repeated percent increase or decrease.

Key Representations

  • Initial values and growth factors
  • Tables of input-output values
  • Graphs with visible intercepts and multiplicative change
  • Context statements with percent growth or decay
  • Equations such as $f(x)=a(1+r)^x$ and $f(x)=a(1-r)^x$

Modeling Opportunities

  • Create savings or investment models with repeated percent growth
  • Create depreciation models for cars, technology, or equipment
  • Translate table values into exponential equations
  • Use an equation to forecast future values within a context
Section 7.3: Graphing and Analyzing Exponential Functions

Learning Target: Students will graph exponential functions and analyze key features.

I Can Statements

  • I can design graphs of exponential functions using key points, growth or decay factors, and asymptote behavior.
  • I can apply key features of exponential graphs to interpret domain, range, intercepts, and end behavior.
  • I can assess whether a graph is reasonable for an exponential equation or situation.

Key Representations

  • Coordinate graphs of exponential functions
  • Tables generated from exponential equations
  • Key points such as the y-intercept
  • Horizontal asymptote behavior
  • Domain and range statements

Modeling Opportunities

  • Graph growth and decay models from equations
  • Connect an equation's parameters to graph behavior
  • Interpret asymptote behavior in real-world settings
  • Use key points to sketch and check exponential graphs
Section 7.4: Comparing Function Families

Learning Target: Students will compare linear, quadratic, and exponential functions using multiple representations.

I Can Statements

  • I can formulate comparisons among linear, quadratic, and exponential functions using rates, differences, ratios, and graph features.
  • I can use tables, graphs, equations, and situations to distinguish additive, quadratic, and multiplicative patterns.
  • I can critique claims about function families using evidence from multiple representations.

Key Representations

  • Tables showing first differences, second differences, and ratios
  • Linear, quadratic, and exponential graphs
  • Equations for multiple function families
  • Context descriptions of change over time
  • Feature comparisons such as intercepts, growth patterns, and turning points

Modeling Opportunities

  • Choose a function family for a data pattern using evidence
  • Contrast additive growth with multiplicative growth
  • Analyze when an exponential model eventually exceeds a linear or quadratic model
  • Revise a model choice after checking multiple representations
Section 7.5: Exponential Modeling with Data

Learning Target: Students will create and analyze exponential models using real-world data.

I Can Statements

  • I can construct exponential models from real-world data using an initial value and a growth or decay factor.
  • I can extend exponential models to make predictions and interpret results in context.
  • I can assess model fit and limitations when exponential data do not follow a perfect pattern.

Key Representations

  • Real-world data tables
  • Scatter plots with exponential trends
  • Exponential equations built from data
  • Predictions and residual reasoning
  • Contextual interpretations of parameters and outputs

Modeling Opportunities

  • Model viral sharing, population change, or compound growth
  • Model decay situations such as medicine concentration or depreciation
  • Use data to estimate a growth or decay factor
  • Check whether a model prediction is reasonable for the context
Unit Modeling Themes
  • Represent repeated multiplication with tables, graphs, equations, and contexts
  • Connect growth and decay factors to percent change
  • Interpret exponential parameters as contextual starting values and multipliers
  • Use multiple representations to distinguish exponential functions from linear and quadratic functions
  • Assess whether exponential models and predictions are reasonable for real data

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, representation recognition, or exit ticket

Purpose: DOK 1 reveals whether students can read exponential representations, use basic vocabulary, and notice repeated multiplicative change before writing or analyzing models.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Growth and Decay Recognition Can Be Assessed After Section 7.1 - Classify situations, sequences, and tables as exponential growth, exponential decay, repeated addition, or neither; use constant ratios and common differences; connect pattern evidence to growth or decay labels.
  2. Multiplier and Initial Value Fluency Can Be Assessed After Section 7.2 - Use equations, percent-change statements, multipliers, and starting values; generate early outputs from an initial value and multiplier; distinguish growth from decay using multiplier size.
  3. Exponential Graph Feature Reading Can Be Assessed After Section 7.3 - Represent exponential equations with tables and plotted key points; read y-intercepts, horizontal asymptotes, and multiplier behavior from graphs and equations; connect vertical shifts to asymptote values.
  4. Function Family Evidence Fluency Can Be Assessed After Section 7.4 - Determine whether first differences, second differences, or ratios best reveal a function family; classify equations and data sets as linear, quadratic, exponential growth, exponential decay, or neither; use evidence beyond surface direction.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 1 performance shows whether students can recognize exponential growth and decay, read multipliers and starting values, interpret basic graph features, and separate exponential evidence from linear or quadratic evidence before deeper modeling.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Mini-quiz, graph/table task, matching task, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: DOK 2 reveals whether students can move among tables, graphs, equations, and contexts while using exponential structure accurately.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Constant Ratio Evidence Can Be Assessed After Section 7.2 - Use tables and contexts to test for constant ratios; find multipliers and initial values for $y=ab^x$; complete growth and decay tables with evidence for exponential decisions.
  2. Writing Exponential Equations Can Be Assessed After Section 7.2 - Build exponential equations from tables, point pairs, and percent-change contexts; calculate multipliers before writing $y=ab^x$; use models to make rounded predictions.
  3. Graphing Exponential Features Can Be Assessed After Section 7.3 - Represent exponential functions with key-point graphs; interpret y-intercepts, horizontal asymptotes, growth or decay, and starting-value changes; compare graphs that share a multiplier.
  4. Model and Family Comparison Can Be Assessed After Section 7.5 - Evaluate linear and exponential models using tables, graphs, and scatter plots; estimate crossover points, starting values, and multipliers; choose reasonable model forms from evidence.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 2 performance shows whether students can apply constant-ratio reasoning across tables, equations, graphs, contexts, and comparisons. The results reveal whether students can build and use exponential models rather than only naming them.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, model critique, strategy comparison, representation analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: DOK 3 reveals whether students can reason with evidence, check model choices, and interpret exponential predictions within meaningful constraints.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Growth and Decay Error Analysis Can Be Assessed After Section 7.4 - Critique growth and decay classifications and incorrect percent multipliers; use ratio and difference tests to correct sequence, table, and model errors; connect corrected models to graph behavior.
  2. Representation Mismatch Analysis Can Be Assessed After Section 7.3 - Assess mismatched tables, graphs, contexts, and asymptote claims; correct values or statements; justify corrections using multipliers, equations, and long-run behavior.
  3. Function Family Critique Can Be Assessed After Section 7.4 - Evaluate claims comparing exponential, linear, and quadratic growth; use multiple input values, differences, and ratios; conclude which family or long-term behavior is supported.
  4. Data Modeling Strategy Can Be Assessed After Section 7.5 - Develop exponential modeling strategies for imperfect data; estimate multipliers from ratios or scatter plots; compare exponential and linear models and state limitations.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 3 performance shows whether students can diagnose errors, reconcile mismatched representations, critique family claims, and justify modeling choices from numerical evidence instead of relying on surface appearance.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or capstone synthesis task

Purpose: DOK 4 reveals whether students can synthesize exponential structure, model data, and make defensible choices across function families in short performance settings.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Growth and Decay Context Design Can Be Assessed After Section 7.2 - Design paired growth and decay contexts and equations from a shared initial value; make predictions from chosen multipliers; create models that meet checkpoint constraints and explain behavior differences.
  2. Exponential Model Revision Can Be Assessed After Section 7.5 - Adapt incorrect or incomplete exponential models; revise percent-change multipliers from contexts and tables; make predictions while noting realistic limitations.
  3. Function Family Evidence Synthesis Can Be Assessed After Section 7.4 - Validate function-family claims using tables and student reasoning; create or revise evidence sets; use differences and ratios to recommend and justify model families.
  4. Exponential Modeling Design Can Be Assessed After Section 7.5 - Model real-world exponential data sets, graph-based contexts, and competing view-count models; write equations and predictions; interpret asymptotes, limitations, and realistic fit.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, revise, and synthesize exponential models in short performance settings. The work shows whether students can connect context, equation, data, prediction, limitation, and function-family evidence into a defensible model choice.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 7 Summative Assessment Late-course summative with spiral review

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of pattern analysis, equation writing, graph interpretation, function-family comparison, and exponential modeling tasks aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize recognizing multiplicative change, writing exponential equations, interpreting graph features, comparing function families across representations, and making contextual predictions from exponential models.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 7 content is revisited through subsequent units via retrieval practice, warm-ups, and formative assessments.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates strong flexible understanding of exponential relationships, equations, graphs, function-family comparisons, and data-based modeling. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of exponential growth and decay, equation writing, graph analysis, and basic model interpretation. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of exponential patterns, equations, graphs, and contextual predictions. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of exponential relationships, function-family comparison, or exponential modeling. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
