Algebra 1 - Unit 3: Systems and Constraints

Essential Standard

Students will solve and interpret systems of equations and inequalities in order to model situations, analyze constraints, and justify solutions using equations, graphs, tables, and contexts.
Section 3.1: Graphing and Elimination

Learning Target: Students will solve systems graphically and by elimination while interpreting the meaning of different solution types.

I Can Statements

  • I can solve a system using a graph.
  • I can solve a system using elimination.
  • I can identify one solution, no solution, and infinitely many solutions.
  • I can explain how graphs and equations show the same solution behavior.
  • I can verify whether an ordered pair satisfies both equations.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Systems of equations
  • Tables
  • Algebraic elimination
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • intersection of two cost plans
  • mixture or combination situations
  • checking solutions with graphs
Section 3.2: Solving Systems by Substitution

Learning Target: Students will solve systems using substitution and choose efficient forms for solving.

I Can Statements

  • I can solve systems using substitution.
  • I can rewrite equations to prepare for substitution.
  • I can verify solutions.
  • I can choose when substitution is an efficient strategy.
  • I can connect substitution steps to a graph or context.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Graphs
  • Tables
  • Substitution expressions
  • Ordered pairs

Modeling Opportunities

  • one equation already solved for a variable
  • cost and quantity relationships
  • comparison of equivalent forms
Section 3.3: Writing and Solving Systems from Situations

Learning Target: Students will create and solve systems that model real-world situations.

I Can Statements

  • I can define variables.
  • I can write equations from situations.
  • I can create a system from a context.
  • I can solve a system and interpret the meaning of the solution.
  • I can decide whether the solution is reasonable in context.

Key Representations

  • Contexts
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Graphs
  • Verbal descriptions

Modeling Opportunities

  • ticket and admission models
  • two-rate work situations
  • sales or fundraising comparisons
  • mixture and count problems
Section 3.4: Graphing Linear Inequalities

Learning Target: Students will graph and interpret linear inequalities.

I Can Statements

  • I can graph linear inequalities.
  • I can determine which region satisfies an inequality.
  • I can use test points to verify solutions.
  • I can interpret inequality solutions in context.
  • I can distinguish solid and dashed boundary lines.

Key Representations

  • Inequalities
  • Graphs
  • Boundary lines
  • Shaded half-planes
  • Test points

Modeling Opportunities

  • spending limits
  • minimum and maximum requirements
  • resource restrictions
Section 3.5: Linear Systems of Inequalities

Learning Target: Students will analyze and interpret systems of linear inequalities.

I Can Statements

  • I can graph systems of inequalities.
  • I can identify the feasible region.
  • I can determine whether a point satisfies all constraints.
  • I can interpret feasible regions in context.
  • I can justify decisions using constraints.

Key Representations

  • Systems of inequalities
  • Graphs
  • Feasible regions
  • Candidate points
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • budget constraints
  • production planning
  • ingredient limits
  • decision-making with restrictions
Unit Modeling Themes
  • intersection as shared solution
  • constraints and feasible choices
  • comparing plans or rates
  • ticket sales and cost models
  • resource limits
  • decision-making with systems
  • graphical and algebraic verification

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, graph identification, ordered-pair check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recognize systems, identify solution behavior from graphs, verify ordered pairs, use system vocabulary, and identify boundary-line or shading features.

Assessment Ideas

  1. System Solution Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students identify system vocabulary, ordered-pair solutions, and solution types.
  2. Substitution and Elimination Readiness Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students identify equations prepared for substitution or elimination and verify simple ordered pairs.
  3. Linear Inequality Graph Features Check Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students identify boundary lines, shading, and test-point meaning.
  4. Constraint Vocabulary and Feasible Point Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify feasible regions, candidate points, and constraints.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need support with the language and visual meaning of systems, constraints, solution sets, and feasibility before moving into solving and modeling.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, mini-quiz, graphing task, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can solve routine systems, graph inequalities, write systems from straightforward situations, and connect graphical and algebraic solution methods.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Graphing and Elimination Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students solve a system by graphing or elimination and verify the solution.
  2. Substitution Solving Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students solve systems using substitution and check ordered pairs.
  3. Writing Systems from Situations Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students define variables, write a system, solve, and interpret the solution.
  4. Graphing Inequalities and Feasible Region Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students graph inequalities, test points, and identify feasible solutions.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally carry out system-solving and graphing procedures. These assessments reveal whether students can organize equations, graphs, and contexts accurately.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, representation analysis, strategy comparison, error analysis, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify solution methods, explain solution behavior, critique incorrect reasoning, and connect algebraic work to graphs and contexts.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Solution Type Justification Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students justify one solution, no solution, or infinitely many solutions using graphs and equations.
  2. Strategy Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students compare graphing, substitution, and elimination for efficiency and meaning.
  3. Model Interpretation and Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students critique a system model or interpretation and revise the reasoning.
  4. Feasible Region Reasoning Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students defend whether points satisfy all constraints and explain the meaning of a feasible region.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand systems and constraints as connected representations rather than isolated procedures. These tasks identify reasoning gaps, misconception patterns, and readiness for more complex modeling.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer systems and constraints to unfamiliar situations, design coherent models, make decisions, and justify conclusions using mathematical evidence.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a System Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students create a situation, write a system, solve it, and explain the solution in context.
  2. Choose the Best Method Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students select and defend a solving strategy for a system based on structure.
  3. Constraint Decision Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students use inequalities and feasible regions to recommend a valid choice.
  4. Critique and Redesign Constraint Model Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students evaluate a flawed system or inequality model and revise it to fit the situation.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, connect, critique, and revise systems and constraints. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 3 Summative Assessment Unit 3 Assessed During Unit 5

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of system-solving tasks, graphing tasks, modeling tasks, inequality tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize graphing and solving systems, substitution, elimination, writing systems from situations, graphing linear inequalities, identifying feasible regions, interpreting constraints, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 3 is assessed during Unit 5 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 3 concepts: systems of equations, substitution, elimination, systems from situations, linear inequalities, and constraints. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 3 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 3 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 3 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
