Algebra 1 - Unit 6: Solving and Modeling with Quadratics

Essential Standard

Students will solve, analyze, and model quadratic relationships in order to interpret solutions, compare representations, and make decisions using equations, graphs, tables, and contexts.
Section 6.1: Solving Quadratics by Factoring

Learning Target: Students will solve quadratic equations using factored form and interpret solutions.

I Can Statements

  • I can solve quadratic equations by factoring.
  • I can apply the zero product property.
  • I can connect solutions to x-intercepts.
  • I can interpret solutions in context.
  • I can verify solutions algebraically or graphically.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Graphs
  • Tables
  • Factored form
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • projectile motion
  • area problems
  • revenue models
  • zero-product contexts
Section 6.2: Completing the Square

Learning Target: Students will solve quadratic equations by completing the square and connect the process to vertex form.

I Can Statements

  • I can complete the square.
  • I can solve quadratic equations using completing the square.
  • I can rewrite standard form into vertex form.
  • I can explain why completing the square works.
  • I can connect completed-square form to the vertex of a graph.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Graphs
  • Diagrams
  • Vertex form
  • Tables

Modeling Opportunities

  • optimization situations
  • projectile motion
  • geometric square diagrams
Section 6.3: Quadratic Formula

Learning Target: Students will solve quadratic equations using the quadratic formula and interpret solutions.

I Can Statements

  • I can identify $a$, $b$, and $c$ in a quadratic equation.
  • I can use the quadratic formula to solve equations.
  • I can determine the number of real solutions.
  • I can interpret solutions in context.
  • I can verify solutions using graphs, tables, or substitution.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Graphs
  • Formula organizers
  • Tables
  • Contexts

Modeling Opportunities

  • projectile motion
  • area optimization
  • situations with non-factorable quadratics
Section 6.4: Choosing and Defending Solution Methods

Learning Target: Students will compare factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula and select efficient solution methods.

I Can Statements

  • I can choose an efficient solution method.
  • I can justify my choice of strategy.
  • I can compare multiple solution methods.
  • I can critique incorrect solutions.
  • I can explain when a method is useful or inefficient.

Key Representations

  • Equations
  • Graphs
  • Tables
  • Strategy comparisons
  • Written explanations

Modeling Opportunities

  • compare solution methods in context
  • error analysis
  • strategy-selection discussions
Section 6.5: Quadratic Modeling and Optimization

Learning Target: Students will use quadratic functions to model real-world situations and interpret maximums, minimums, and intercepts.

I Can Statements

  • I can create a quadratic model from data.
  • I can interpret the vertex in context.
  • I can interpret intercepts in context.
  • I can use a quadratic model to answer questions and make predictions.
  • I can evaluate whether a quadratic model is reasonable.

Key Representations

  • Graphs
  • Equations
  • Tables
  • Contexts
  • Data displays

Modeling Opportunities

  • ball toss analysis
  • projectile motion
  • launch experiments
  • area optimization

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 - Rungs 1-2

Format: 3-5 minute warm-up, vocabulary check, graph-feature check, formula identification, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, identify equation features, recognize graph features, identify $a$, $b$, and $c$, and connect solutions to zeros or intercepts.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Zero Product Property Check Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students identify factored equations, possible zeros, and when the Zero Product Property applies.
  2. Completing the Square Vocabulary Check Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students identify square structure, vertex form, and completing-the-square components.
  3. Quadratic Formula Setup Check Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students identify $a$, $b$, $c$, and the discriminant expression without completing the full solve.
  4. Quadratic Model Feature Check Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students identify roots, intercepts, vertices, maximums, minimums, and context features.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need support with notation, vocabulary, graph features, and recognizing when a solution method is available before deeper quadratic reasoning can occur.
DOK 2 - Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, mini-quiz, graph/equation task, method-practice task, partner check, or short written task

Purpose: Check whether students can solve quadratic equations using factoring, completing the square, the quadratic formula, and routine graph/model interpretation.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Solving by Factoring Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students solve by factoring and verify using substitution or graph features.
  2. Completing the Square Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students complete the square, solve, and connect the result to vertex form.
  3. Quadratic Formula Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students use the quadratic formula to solve a quadratic equation and interpret the number of real solutions.
  4. Quadratic Model Interpretation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students use a graph or equation to answer routine modeling questions.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally execute solution methods and read quadratic models, but the assessment reveals whether they connect algebraic solutions to graphs and contexts.
DOK 3 - Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, strategy comparison, error analysis, model interpretation, or discourse prompt

Purpose: Check whether students can justify solution methods, critique errors, compare strategies, and explain how solutions, vertices, and intercepts connect across representations.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Zero Product Reasoning Task Can Be Assessed After Section 1 - Students explain why the equation must equal zero before using the Zero Product Property.
  2. Completing the Square Explanation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 2 - Students justify the square-completion step and connect it to graph features.
  3. Method Choice Defense Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students compare factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula for efficiency and reliability.
  4. Quadratic Model Critique Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students critique an interpretation of roots, vertices, or predictions in context.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students understand quadratic solving as connected reasoning rather than a collection of disconnected procedures.
DOK 4 - Rungs 7-8

Format: Brief performance task, modeling task, critique-and-revise task, design task, or extended application

Purpose: Check whether students can transfer quadratic-solving tools to unfamiliar situations, select appropriate methods, build models, and justify decisions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Design a Quadratic Equation Task Can Be Assessed After Section 3 - Students design equations requiring different solution methods and justify why each method fits.
  2. Critique and Revise a Solution Method Task Can Be Assessed After Section 4 - Students critique a flawed method choice or algebraic solution and revise the reasoning.
  3. Projectile or Area Model Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students interpret roots, vertex, and constraints in a short contextual model.
  4. Quadratic Decision-Making Task Can Be Assessed After Section 5 - Students use a quadratic model to make and justify a recommendation.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can design, critique, model, and make decisions with quadratic relationships. These should remain short performance tasks rather than large projects.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 6 Summative Assessment Unit 6 Assessed During Unit 8

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of quadratic solving tasks, graph interpretation tasks, method-selection tasks, modeling tasks, and short reasoning prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize solving by factoring, completing the square, using the quadratic formula, interpreting the discriminant, choosing efficient methods, connecting roots and intercepts, interpreting vertices, quadratic modeling, mathematical communication, and justification of reasoning.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1-3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, critique, design, and performance tasks are assessed separately through formative assessments, collaborative activities, investigations, discourse tasks, and performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative assessment.

Because of the spiraling course structure, Unit 6 is assessed during Unit 8 after students have had additional opportunities to revisit and strengthen understanding through retrieval practice, warm-ups, formative assessments, and subsequent instruction.

Holistic Rubric

A - Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 6 concepts: solving quadratic equations, selecting efficient methods, interpreting solutions, and modeling quadratic situations. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student consistently connects equations, graphs, tables, and contexts while justifying conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B - Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 6 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth.

C - Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 6 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with notation, reasoning, restrictions, representations, or multi-step problem solving. Work may contain errors or incomplete explanations, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet - Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 6 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, weak conceptual understanding, or inability to connect representations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
